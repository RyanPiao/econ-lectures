§1  Optimization Under Scarcity

10 min · The template that drives every chapter

Every micro problem starts here

Scarcity forces choice

  • Student: 168 hours/week
  • Household: a paycheck
  • Firm: fixed capital stock
  • Government: budget cap

In Principles, scarcity was a premise . In 2316, it’s the structure .

The Template

$$\max_{x \in X} f(x) \;\;\text{s.t.}\;\; g(x) \le 0$$

Choose $x$ from $X$ to make $f(x)$ as large as possible, subject to constraint $g$.

Memorize the structure — not because it’s hard, but because every chapter just relabels $f$, $x$, $g$.

Problem

Maya has 97 free hrs/wk ; wage = $18/hr . Study value declines:

Hours studying MV (per hour)
1–10 $30
11–20 $20
21–30 $15
31+ $0

How should she split her 97 hours?

An individual time budget and a billion-dollar policy budget — both fit $\max f(x) \;\text{s.t.}\; g(x) \le 0$.

$x$: hours allocated to study

§2  Equilibrium & the HPAI Egg Shock

14 min · Mutual best-response, not "system at rest"

Equilibrium = mutual best-response

Wrong intuition

"Equilibrium" = a system at rest because nothing is happening.

Right intuition

Each agent’s action is already the optimal response to every other agent’s action. No one wants to change.

Shock it → agents re-optimize against each other’s new best moves → we land at a new mutual best-response.

The interesting questions

Which way does the system move? How fast does it return?

We’ll meet this idea again as Nash equilibrium in Ch 14.

The U.S. Egg Market 2022–2025

HPAI — highly pathogenic avian influenza — forced the culling of ~168 million laying hens from 2022 to early 2025.

Pullets take 18–20 weeks to reach laying age → supply curve shifted sharply inward in the short run.

Demand barely moved — eggs are a staple with few breakfast substitutes.

Jan 2025 : $4.95/dozen (FRED APU0000708111 ) — +96% YoY

FRED APU0000708111 — the data

When demand is steep, the shock lands mostly on price — not quantity.

  Try it — press 3 for a 3-min timer. Find $P_0^*, Q_0^*, P_1^*, Q_1^*$.

Problem

Linear S&D for the egg market:

$Q_d = 200 - 5P$

$Q_s = 50 + 10P$

HPAI shifts supply inward by 30 at every price:

$\tilde Q_s = 20 + 10P$

Find pre- and post-shock equilibrium.

Equilibrium = market clearing. Set $Q_d = Q_s$, solve for $P^*$, plug back for $Q^*$. Repeat with the new supply.

Solve

  • Post: $200 - 5P = 20 + 10P \Rightarrow P_1^* = \$12$, $Q_1^* = 140$

$\Delta P = +\$2$ (+20%) · $\Delta Q = -10$ (-6.7%)

Of 30 "lost" units, 20 are clawed back via the price response.

§3  Marginal Thinking & the FOC

11 min · The mindset change that needs calculus

Marginal = derivative

Principles

Marginal cost = cost of the next discrete unit :

$C(q) - C(q-1)$

Slope of a secant line .

2316

Shrink the secant horizontally to zero → tangent line:

$$MC(q) = \frac{dC(q)}{dq}$$

Slope of the tangent .

"At what $q$ should the firm stop?" becomes "at what $q$ is $dR/dq - dC/dq = 0$?" — a one-line calculus problem .

Tangent (marginal) vs chord (average)

The tangent slope ( marginal ) and the chord slope ( average ) are different objects .

HeadsUp 1.3: using one when you want the other is the most common exam-day error. Average tells history; marginal tells the next decision.

Poll #3 in 5 minutes will hammer this trap.

CalculusCorner 1.1 — First-Order Condition

For a differentiable $f(x)$, an interior maximum or minimum $x^*$ on an open interval satisfies

$$f'(x^*) = 0.$$

Geometry: at the top of a smooth hill, the tangent is horizontal.

Necessary, not sufficient. SOC distinguishes max from min: $f''(x^*) \le 0$ (max), $\ge 0$ (min).

Quick example: $\pi(q) = 10q - 0.5q^2$ → $\pi'(q) = 10 - q = 0$ → $q^* = 10$, $\pi(10) = 50$.

Poll #3: Marginal vs Average

A firm has total revenue \$1,000 and total cost \$800 at $q = 100$. At $q = 100$: $MR = 4$, $MC = 5$ (\$/unit).

Should the firm produce the 101st unit?

A) Yes — profitable on average (\$1,000 > \$800)
B) Yes — total revenue exceeds total cost
C) No — at $q = 100$, $MR < MC$
D) Not enough information

60 sec to vote

Highest-error concept in the chapter. Spend the full debrief minute on it.

Answer: C — the next unit loses $1

(A) and (B) are both true statements about history — yes, profitable on average. Neither answers the question. At the margin: $MR = 4 < MC = 5$ . Make the unit, lose $1.

Tape this to your notebook: Average tells you about history . Marginal tells you about the next decision .

§4  Models — Maps, Not Photos

8 min · A model is useful because it leaves things out

Amazon’s Millionth Warehouse Robot

Mid-2025: Amazon crosses one million warehouse robots — machines now move shelves alongside human workers across its fulfillment network.

It’s a classic firm-level optimization problem: pick the mix of capital (robots, conveyors) and labor (pickers, loaders) that ships the same orders at the lowest cost. As wages rise and capital gets cheaper, the mix shifts.

We’ll formalize this in Ch 7–8.

HeadsUp 1.4 — Models are tools, not portraits

When this course assumes perfect competition , rational choice , or constant returns to scale , that’s a baseline assumption — not a claim every market actually works that way.

The right question is not :

"Is this assumption true?" (Almost none are, strictly.)

It’s:

"Does relaxing this assumption change the result I care about?"

Poll #4: Which Assumption is Load-Bearing?

Amazon model: $q = f(K, L)$ s.t. $wL + rK \le C$.

Which assumption is load-bearing for the qualitative prediction "as $w$ rises, more robots"?

A) Constant returns to scale
B) Smooth substitution between $K$ and $L$
C) Perfect competition in input markets
D) No hiring frictions

60 sec

Answer: B — smooth substitution

For the qualitative direction (wage up → robots up), substitution at the margin must be possible. Perfect complements (Leontief) gives no substitution; perfect substitutes gives a corner.

(A) and (C) sharpen the answer but aren’t load-bearing for the direction . Cross out an assumption mentally and ask: would the punchline still hold?

§5  The Empirical Turn

16 min · Counterfactuals, RCTs, difference-in-differences

The fundamental problem of causal inference

The causal effect of $X$ on $Y$ for a unit is the difference between $Y$ with $X$ and $Y$ without $X$, holding everything else constant.

The problem: only one of those two outcomes can ever be observed for the same unit at the same time.

HeadsUp 1.5: "States with more teachers per student have higher test scores." This is a correlation . The causal interpretation — "hiring more teachers raises scores" — doesn’t follow without more work.

Habit: when you see "$X$ is associated with $Y$," ask what is the counterfactual, and how was it constructed?

Correlation ≠ Causation

Correlation

$X$ and $Y$ move together in the data. That’s all.

Causation

Changing $X$ makes $Y$ change — holding everything else constant.

A correlation can come from causation , reverse causation , a hidden confounder , or pure chance .

Bridge slide between the counterfactual definition and the RCT/DiD strategies. Use the image to walk through the four sources of correlation (causal X→Y, reverse Y→X, confounding Z→X & Z→Y, chance), then say: "the rest of §5 is about strategies for ruling out everything except the first."

Walkthrough 1.5 — Why Did the MTA Pick $9?

  Try it — press 3 . Find $R(9)$, $R(12)$, then the revenue-max $P^*$.

Problem

Pre-toll daily demand for Manhattan CBD entries:

$Q_d(P) = 300{,}000 - 5{,}000 P$

Find:

  1. Revenue at $P = \$9$
  2. Revenue at $P = \$12$
  3. Revenue-maximizing toll $P^*$
  4. Why did MTA pick $\$9$, not $P^*$?

Set up

$R(P) = P \cdot Q_d(P) = 300{,}000P - 5{,}000P^2$. Differentiate, set $R'(P) = 0$ for the max.

Solve

  • $R(9) = 9 \cdot 255{,}000 \approx \$2.30$ M/day
  • $R(12) = 12 \cdot 240{,}000 \approx \$2.88$ M/day
  • $R'(P) = 300{,}000 - 10{,}000P = 0 \Rightarrow P^* = \$30$

$R(30) \approx \$4.5$ M/day (revenue-max)

Why MTA picked \$9, not \$30: revenue isn’t the only objective. At \$30, entries fall to 150K (half) → political revolt. The MTA solved a constrained problem.

Key Takeaways

Optimization is the spine. Every chapter just relabels $f$, $x$, $g$.

Marginal = derivative. $f'(x^*) = 0$ opens every interior optimization.

Equilibrium is mutual best-response, not "system at rest." Positive, not normative.

Models are purposeful simplifications. Ask "load-bearing?" not "true?"

Causal claims require a counterfactual. RCTs build it; DiD finds one in nature. "Correlation is not causation" is a methodology promise, not a slogan.
