Section 1

From Traditional ML
to Language Models

What happens when you remove the target variable?

LLMs Are Conditional Probability Machines

Every model you built (OLS, RF, DML) had:

  • Defined features X and target y
  • A loss function you chose
  • Held-out evaluation data

LLMs break this pattern. Trained on billions of tokens to predict the next word. No explicit features, no target you designed.

Next-Token Prediction

P(wt+1 | w1, w2, ..., wt)

The model doesn't "know" anything — it has learned which words tend to follow other words. Stochastic parrot (Bender et al., 2021).

Emphasize: the model has no mechanism to verify factual accuracy. It produces text that looks like training data. Sometimes correct, sometimes not.

Embeddings: From TF-IDF to Dense Vectors

Ch 23 callback: same idea (text as numbers), much richer representation

TF-IDF (Ch 23)

  • Sparse — mostly zeros
  • One dim per vocab word (~50K)
  • "Fed raised rates" ≠ "Central bank tightened"

Embeddings

  • Dense — every dimension carries info
  • Fixed size (384–1536)
  • "Fed raised rates" ≈ "Central bank tightened"
This matters because embeddings are the foundation of RAG — the technique that makes AI output trustworthy.

Class Poll

An LLM generates: "US GDP grew 3.1% in Q4 2024."
What should you do FIRST?

A. Trust it — LLMs are trained on real data
B. Google the exact number
C. Check the BEA source release directly
D. Ask the LLM to cite its source

C is correct. Option D is the trap — remember Mata v. Avianca. Asking an LLM for citations is like asking a witness for their own alibi.

After reveal: D is tempting but the citation itself may be hallucinated. Primary source verification is the only reliable approach.

Think-Pair-Share

You are writing a policy brief on Federal Reserve communications. Which would you trust more:

Your Ch 23 TF-IDF classifier

No single right answer. TF-IDF is auditable; GPT-4 captures nuance but may hallucinate. The ideal answer: "it depends on verification requirements." This sets up RAG as the middle path.

Section 2

AI-Assisted Development

The Productivity Paradox

The AI Coding Tool Spectrum

Autocomplete

Predicts next tokens as you type. df.groupby('state') → .agg({'income': 'median'})

Chat

Answers code questions. "How do I compute HC3 robust SEs?"

Agentic

Plans + executes multi-step tasks. "Download FRED data, merge, regress, plot"

Agentic mode: largest productivity gains and highest risks. Subtle errors propagate silently.

Transition: "Let's see what the evidence actually says about productivity. The results will surprise you."

The Perception Gap

Peng et al. (2023) RCT: Developers with Copilot completed tasks 55.8% faster. Well-defined, modular tasks.

METR (2025) RCT: 16 expert developers on complex codebases. AI tools made them 19% slower (95% CI: 2%–39%).

But they believed they were 20% faster.

Perception-reality gap: ~40 pp

This is Chapter 9 logic: RCT design, random assignment. The perception gap is sampling bias from Ch 6 — you notice the 5 minutes AI saved, not the 15 minutes debugging.

Class Poll

The METR study found expert devs were 19% slower with AI tools. What best explains this?

A. AI tools are poorly designed for complex tasks
B. Generated code was subtly wrong, debugging took longer
C. Devs relied on AI instead of their own codebase knowledge
D. Sample of 16 developers was too small

Both B and C are defensible — complementary explanations. D is undermined by the tight confidence interval.

Key insight: the perception gap means you cannot trust your intuition about AI productivity. You need to measure — same principle as Ch 9 RCTs.

These numbers make the problem concrete. The shift from banning to deploying responsibly is the key narrative.

How RAG Works: Four Stages

1. Chunk

Split docs into 200–500 token passages

2. Embed

Dense vectors via embedding model → ChromaDB

3. Retrieve

Find k most similar chunks to question

4. Generate

LLM answers from context, not memory

retrieved = argmaxd ∈ D sim(embed(q), embed(d))

Progressive build: reveal one stage at a time. The retrieval equation connects to Ch 23 — same similarity logic, different representation.

Evaluating RAG: RAGAS Metrics

Es et al. (2023) — Same precision logic from Ch 18

Faithfulness

Does the answer contain only claims supported by retrieved context?

Answer Relevancy

Does the answer actually address the question?

Context Precision@k

Are relevant chunks ranked at the top?

For economic applications, faithfulness is most critical — every claim must trace to an actual document.

Connect to Ch 18: same precision/recall logic, applied to document retrieval. Students have seen this framework before.

Class Poll

In a RAG pipeline, what causes hallucination DESPITE having relevant documents?

A. The embedding model is too small
B. The LLM generates beyond what retrieved chunks support
C. The vector database has too few dimensions
D. The chunk size is too large

B — The LLM is still a next-token predictor. It can go off-script even with grounded context. That's why "Based ONLY on the following excerpts" matters.

This is the key conceptual point: RAG reduces hallucination but doesn't eliminate it. Human review remains necessary.

Decision Scenario

Your team has 10,000 pages of FOMC minutes (30 years). You need quarterly analysis of inflation language evolution.

Option A: RAG

  • Monthly cost: $350–$2,850
  • Adding new minutes: near-zero
  • Setup: 1–2 weeks
  • Citable sources: yes

Option B: Fine-Tune

  • Per iteration: $10K–$50K
  • Retraining per update: $500–$5K
  • Setup: 4–8 weeks
  • Citable sources: no

Show of hands: RAG or Fine-Tune? Defend your choice.

RAG wins for evolving corpora. Accept fine-tuning arguments for fixed historical analysis. Key: students reason about cost-flexibility tradeoff.

Section 4

AI Agents & Ethics

Human-in-the-Loop • Goodhart's Law Revisited

AI Agents: Plan → Act → Observe

ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2023)

Thought: I need to download CPI data from FRED.
Action: python_execute("cpi = pdr.get_data_fred('CPIAUCSL')")
Observation: DataFrame with 180 rows

Thought: Now compute year-over-year inflation.
Action: python_execute("cpi['inflation'] = cpi.pct_change(12)*100")
Observation: Column 'inflation' added.

Caution: Agents can automate data pipelines, but cannot determine if your identification strategy is valid. Automate the mechanical steps; retain judgment for economic reasoning.

DML nuisance parameter estimation (Ch 24) is a good candidate for automation; choosing instrumental variables is not.

Human-in-the-Loop & Goodhart's Law

ECB Model (2026)

LLMs score Corporate Telephone Survey. Every AI score reviewed by an economist before entering official data. Strong AI-human correlation (MAD < 0.5).

Epistemic Cost

Anthropic (2025): AI users scored 17% lower on mastery quizzes. AI made tasks easier to complete but harder to learn from.

Goodhart's Law (Ch 2 callback): If you optimize your RAG pipeline for its faithfulness score, you may get technically faithful (every claim traces to a document) but economically meaningless output. Metrics are tools, not goals.

Goodhart's Law callback: Ch 2 → Ch 18 → Ch 25. Same principle, three domains. The economist's role: ensure meaning behind metrics is preserved.

Key Takeaways

1

LLMs are pattern matchers, not fact-checkers. Hallucination rates reach 41% in finance.

2

AI productivity is context-dependent. 55.8% faster (modular) vs 19% slower (complex) — with a 40pp perception gap.

3

RAG grounds AI in evidence. Chunk → Embed → Retrieve → Generate. Your documents become the model's evidence base.

4

Human-in-the-loop is mandatory. AI scores, humans verify. JPMorgan: 70% time reduction, zero analyst elimination.

5

56% wage premium rewards AI use, not AI knowledge. Delegate syntax, retain judgment.
