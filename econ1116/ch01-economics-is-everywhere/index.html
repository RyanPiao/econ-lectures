for a ride that costs $14 during the day

What Happens Next?

Demand drops

Some riders decide $52 isn't worth it. They split a cab, call a friend, or wait for the T.

What Is Economics?

LO 1.1: Define economics and explain why scarcity forces choices

Two Definitions That Change Everything

Key Concept

Economics

The study of how individuals, firms, and societies make choices under scarcity

Key Concept

Scarcity

Human wants exceed the resources available to satisfy them — forces choices

Common Misconception: "Economics is about money."

Money is one resource. The most important scarce resources are often time, attention, health, and environmental quality.

Scarcity is NOT rarity. Diamonds are rare, but scarcity is universal. Jeff Bezos has billions but only 24 hours. Time is scarce for everyone. The key insight: scarcity forces choices. If you could have everything, you'd never choose. But you can't, so you do.

Scarcity in the 2020s

💊

GLP-1 Drugs

Demand exploded, FDA shortage list. Scarcity of molecules, not money.

🤖

AI Computing

$25K per GPU chip. Training costs $100M+. Even money can't guarantee access.

🏠

Boston Housing

1% vacancy rate vs. 6% national avg. Dozens competing per listing.

💧

Water

Colorado River serves 40M people across 7 states — and it's drying up.

Pattern: Scarcity isn't just about money. It's about molecules, chips, apartments, and water. It's about anything where wants exceed supply.

Rapid-fire through these. Don't deep-dive any one example. The point is showing scarcity in four completely different domains. Transition: "Scarcity shows up everywhere. But here's the thing — it also shows up in YOUR life every day."

Section 2

Opportunity Cost

LO 1.2: Calculate the opportunity cost of a decision using real examples

The Road Not Taken

Key Concept

Opportunity Cost

The value of the next-best alternative you give up when you make a choice

Explicit Costs

Direct out-of-pocket payments

Implicit Costs

Value of opportunities forgone

OC = Explicit + Implicit

Every choice has a cost, even when no money leaves your pocket. The full cost is what you pay PLUS what you give up. Let's make this concrete with an example every student in this room knows.

Worked Example: Campus Coffee

It's 8:15 AM. The line is 15 minutes. A latte costs $4.50. You earn $20/hour.

Step 1: Explicit cost

$4.50

Step 2: Implicit cost (15 min × $20/hr)

$5.00

True opportunity cost

$4.50 + $5.00 = $9.50

Do steps 1 and 2 on the board FIRST. Let students calculate. Then reveal the chart showing the full breakdown. Key point: the implicit cost is LARGER than what you pay. "Once you see costs this way, you'll never look at a line the same way again." Note that OC is personal: student at $12/hr faces different math than student at $25/hr.

The Opportunity Cost of College

$87K

NEU sticker price/year

$35K

Forgone wages/year

Total OC of a bachelor's (public univ.)

~$252,000

Is it worth it?

$1.2M lifetime earnings premium (Georgetown CEW)

42.5% of recent grads are underemployed (NY Fed)

Trade school enrollment +20% since 2020 as students recalculate

This hits close to home. The biggest hidden cost of college isn't tuition — it's what you could be earning instead. The $252K figure includes 4 years of forgone wages. Is it worth it? Usually yes, but not always. 42.5% underemployment is real. Trade schools surging. The point isn't that college is right or wrong — it's that you can't evaluate the decision without accounting for what you're giving up.

Section 3

Thinking at the Margin

LO 1.3: Apply marginal thinking to everyday decisions

Should I Do a Little More?

Key Concept

Marginal Benefit

The additional benefit from one more unit

Key Concept

Marginal Cost

The additional cost of one more unit

The Rule: Keep going as long as MB > MC. Stop when MB = MC.

"Should I go to college?" is all-or-nothing. "Should I take one more course?" is marginal — and much easier to answer.

Economists rarely think all-or-nothing. They ask: should I do a LITTLE more or a little less? This is fundamentally different from categorical thinking. The rule is elegant: keep going as long as the additional benefit exceeds the additional cost.

How Much Should You Study?

HoursExtra Points (MB)
1st+15
2nd+10
3rd+5
4th+2
5th+0

Diminishing marginal returns — each hour adds fewer points

Reveal one row at a time. Ask students to predict: "How many points will the third hour add?" Notice the pattern: diminishing returns. At some point between hour 3 and 4, the marginal benefit drops below the marginal cost. The optimal strategy isn't study as much as possible — it's study until the next hour isn't worth it.

Disney+ Price Hike: Marginal Thinking in Business

In 2023, Disney raised Disney+ by $3 ($10.99 → $13.99). About 5.5% canceled.

+$450M/mo from price increase

−$116M/mo from 8.3M cancellations

Net: +$334M/month

Disney didn't ask "should we charge for streaming?" — that's all-or-nothing. They asked "should we charge $3 MORE?" — that's marginal thinking. The marginal revenue far exceeded the marginal loss. Businesses that think at the margin outperform those that think in absolutes. We'll see this in Ch9, Ch10, Ch11.

Section 4

Incentives

LO 1.4: Identify how incentives shape behavior and predict unintended consequences

People Respond to Incentives

Key Concept

Incentive

Anything that motivates a person to take a particular action — rewards (positive) and penalties (negative)

Back to Uber: Surge pricing creates two incentives simultaneously

Negative incentive for riders

Higher price → some riders drop out

Positive incentive for drivers

Higher price → off-duty drivers return

One price, two incentives, both working toward balance. Elegant — but incentives don't always work this cleanly.

This is our second visit to Uber. Same story, but now we're seeing it through the incentives lens. One price change creates two incentives that push in the same direction — toward equilibrium. Transition: "But incentives don't always produce the effects we expect. Let me tell you about cobras."

The Cobra Effect

British colonial India: government offers a bounty for every dead cobra to reduce the snake population.

Phase 1: It works! People hunt cobras for the reward.

Phase 2: Entrepreneurs start breeding cobras to collect the bounty.

Phase 3: Government cancels the program. Breeders release their snakes.

Result: More cobras than before. The incentive backfired completely.

Progressive build: Phase 1 (works), Phase 2 (gaming begins), Phase 3 (backfire). Pause at each phase. Modern examples: Wells Fargo fake accounts, academic publish-or-perish. The lesson: incentives are essential, but designing them requires thinking several steps ahead.

Predict the Cobra Effect

A city has a rat problem. The government offers $10 per dead rat.
Six months later, what happens?

A

Rats decrease significantly

B

Rats stay about the same

C

Rat population increases

D

People start breeding rats

Have students commit BEFORE revealing. "Raise your hand for A... B... C... D." Most will predict A. Then reveal: "If you said D, you've predicted the cobra effect. And yes — this actually happened in Hanoi with rats." The surprise makes the concept stick.

Answer: D — People Start Breeding Rats

This actually happened in colonial Hanoi. Officials found rat farms on the city outskirts.

The cobra effect: When a bounty creates an incentive to produce the problem rather than solve it. The policy made rats valuable — so people responded rationally by breeding them.

Key insight: Incentives are essential, but designing them requires thinking several steps ahead. People respond to the incentive you create, not the one you intend.

Section 5

Trade-offs

LO 1.5: Efficiency vs. equity — the deepest trade-off in economics

The Deepest Trade-off

Efficiency

Getting the most from scarce resources — making the pie as big as possible

Equity

Distributing resources fairly — making sure everyone gets a fair slice

The tension: Policies that maximize the pie may leave some with tiny slices. Policies that equalize slices may shrink the pie. Neither is automatically better — choosing requires values, not just math.

This is exposure level only — we go deep in Chapters 6 and 7. The key message: efficiency and equity often pull in opposite directions. A progressive tax redistributes (equity) but may reduce work incentives (efficiency). Neither side wins automatically. Economics clarifies the trade-offs; choosing between them is a value judgment.

Rent Control: A Natural Experiment

Nov 2021: St. Paul voters approve a strict 3% rent cap. Minneapolis — just across the river — has no cap.

Equity win: Rents grew only 0.7% for current tenants

Efficiency cost: Building permits dropped 79%. Developers fled to Minneapolis.

This is the textbook efficiency-equity trade-off. St. Paul helped current tenants (equity) but crushed new construction (efficiency). Minneapolis serves as a natural control group — same market, different policy. We'll study rent control in depth in Chapter 7. Key takeaway: trade-offs are inescapable.

The Allocation Game

Form groups of 3-4. Each group picks one scenario:

Scenario A

100 flu vaccines for 200 students. Who gets vaccinated?

Scenario B

50 parking spots for 300 commuters. How do you allocate?

Scenario C

3 seminar seats for 20 waitlisted students. Who gets in?

1. Choose an allocation method  |  2. Name 2 winners and 2 losers  |  3. Is your method more efficient or more equitable?

4 min group work → 2 min debrief

Let groups self-select or assign scenarios. Common methods: first-come-first-served, lottery, price, merit, need-based. During debrief, call on one group per scenario. Key insight: "Every method involves a trade-off. There's no perfect allocation method — only different trade-offs. That's the most important lesson of Chapter 1."
