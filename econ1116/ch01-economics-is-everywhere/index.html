The $52 Uber Ride

It's 2 AM on a Saturday in Boston. You open the Uber app and your jaw drops:

$52

for a ride that costs $14 during the day

"That's highway robbery!" your friend says. But is it?

Paint the scene vividly. Thousands leaving bars, most drivers gone home. Too many riders, too few cars. Pause on the $52 number — let it sink in. Then click to reveal the friend's quote. Don't answer "but is it?" yet — that's the mystery for the lecture.

What Happens Next?

Quantity demanded falls

Some riders decide $52 isn't worth it. They split a cab, call a friend, or wait for the T.

Quantity supplied rises

Off-duty drivers see the surge and get back on the road. Within 20 minutes, quantity supplied catches up with quantity demanded.

The $52 ride isn't random. It's the result of scarcity — too many riders, too few cars. The price rises until supply and demand balance.

We'll return to this Uber example three more times today: scarcity, incentives, and efficiency.

Scarcity You Can Feel: Boston Housing

If you've tried to rent an apartment near Northeastern, you know this one viscerally.

~1%

Boston vacancy rate

~6%

National average

The MBTA Communities Act (2021) requires towns near transit to zone for multifamily housing — increasing the supply of buildable land. But zoning changes take years to become actual apartments.

In the meantime, you're competing with dozens of applicants for every listing.

Efficiency: More housing near transit → shorter commutes, higher productivity, better land use

Fairness: Existing homeowners lose quiet neighborhoods & property values; new residents gain access to housing they couldn't afford before

This one is personal for NEU students. 1% vacancy vs 6% national — that's why you're paying what you're paying. The MBTA Communities Act creates a real trade-off: more housing is efficient (better land use, shorter commutes) but disrupts existing neighborhoods (fairness to current residents vs new residents). Preview of the efficiency-equity tension we'll formalize later.

Section 2

Opportunity Cost

LO 1.2: Calculate the opportunity cost of a decision using real examples

The Road Not Taken

Key Concept

Opportunity Cost

The value of the next-best alternative you give up when you make a choice

Explicit Costs

Direct out-of-pocket payments

Implicit Costs

Value of opportunities forgone

OC = Explicit + Implicit

Every choice has a cost, even when no money leaves your pocket. The full cost is what you pay PLUS what you give up. Let's make this concrete with an example every student in this room knows.

Worked Example: Campus Coffee

It's 8:15 AM. The line is 15 minutes . A latte costs $4.50 . You earn $20/hour .

Step 1: Explicit cost

$4.50

Step 2: Implicit cost (15 min × $20/hr)

$5.00

True opportunity cost

$4.50 + $5.00 = $9.50

Do steps 1 and 2 on the board FIRST. Let students calculate. Then reveal the chart showing the full breakdown. Key point: the implicit cost is LARGER than what you pay. "Once you see costs this way, you'll never look at a line the same way again." Note that OC is personal: student at $12/hr faces different math than student at $25/hr.

Why We Neglect Opportunity Cost

Frederick et al. (2009)

Showed consumers a $1,000 stereo. One group saw an added note: "If you don't buy it, you'll have $1,000 to spend on other things."

Result: Just making the opportunity cost explicit dramatically changed willingness to buy.

Maguire et al. (2023) Meta-Analysis

39 studies, 14,000+ participants

Effect size d = 0.22: People systematically neglect opportunity cost. We don't naturally think about what we're giving up.

This is exactly why economics teaches opportunity cost as the first major concept . Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

Two landmark studies confirm what we intuitively know: people forget about the hidden costs. Frederick showed that simply MENTIONING what you give up changes behavior. The meta-analysis confirms it across 39 studies. This is why we teach it first.

Section 3

Thinking at the Margin

LO 1.3: Apply marginal thinking to everyday decisions

Should I Do a Little More?

Key Concept

Marginal Benefit

The additional benefit from one more unit

Key Concept

Marginal Cost

The additional cost of one more unit

The Rule: Keep going as long as MB > MC. Stop when MB = MC.

"Should I go to college?" is all-or-nothing. "Should I take one more course?" is marginal — and much easier to answer.

Economists rarely think all-or-nothing. They ask: should I do a LITTLE more or a little less? This is fundamentally different from categorical thinking. The rule is elegant: keep going as long as the additional benefit exceeds the additional cost.

How Much Should You Study?

Hours Extra Points (MB)
1st +15
2nd +10
3rd +5
4th +2
5th +0

Diminishing marginal returns — each hour adds fewer points

Reveal one row at a time. Ask students to predict: "How many points will the third hour add?" Notice the pattern: diminishing returns. At some point between hour 3 and 4, the marginal benefit drops below the marginal cost. The optimal strategy isn't study as much as possible — it's study until the next hour isn't worth it.

Disney+ Price Hike: Marginal Thinking in Business

In 2023, Disney raised Disney+ by $3 ($10.99 → $13.99). About 5.5% canceled.

+$450M/mo from price increase

−$116M/mo from 8.3M cancellations

Net: +$334M/month

Disney didn't ask "should we charge for streaming?" — that's all-or-nothing. They asked "should we charge $3 MORE?" — that's marginal thinking. The marginal revenue far exceeded the marginal loss. Businesses that think at the margin outperform those that think in absolutes. We'll see this in Ch9, Ch10, Ch11.

Marginal Thinking Is Everywhere

Subscription Fatigue

Streaming churn hit 44% in Q4 2024. Every month you ask: "Is one more month worth $15.99?" When the answer flips to no, you cancel.

That's marginal analysis — not "should streaming exist?" but "should I keep THIS one?"

Diamond-Water Paradox

Water is essential. Diamonds are decorative. So why are diamonds more expensive?

Answer: Prices reflect marginal value, not total value. Water is abundant — the next glass isn't very valuable. Diamonds are rare — the next one is.

We'll formalize this in Ch8 (marginal utility).

Two more applications. Subscription fatigue: $492B subscription economy, 44% churn — pure marginal analysis at your kitchen table. Diamond-water paradox: one of the oldest puzzles in economics, resolved by marginal thinking. Total value ≠ marginal value.

Shrinkflation: A Marginal Trick

Instead of raising the $3.99 price, companies shrink the package from 100g to 90g.

Price per gram rises , but the sticker price stays the same.

Why it works: Consumers watch total price ($3.99 — same!), not marginal price (per gram — higher). A gap between economic and behavioral reasoning.

We'll return to these behavioral insights in Ch18.

Shrinkflation exploits the gap between how economists think (marginal price per gram) and how consumers think (total sticker price). It's a behavioral trick — and a great bridge to Ch18.

Airline Pricing: Marginal Thinking at 35,000 Feet

An empty seat about to depart has zero value — the ultimate perishable good.

Marginal cost of one more passenger: a few dollars in fuel, a bag of pretzels, and a can of soda.

A $400 seat drops to $89 an hour before departure. As long as fare > marginal cost, the airline profits.

McKinsey: $45B in value from better dynamic pricing over 5 years — 2-3% revenue uplift = up to 15% of EBITDA.

Airlines are the ultimate marginal thinkers. Empty seat = zero value once the door closes. So any fare above the marginal cost of pretzels + fuel is profit. The entire business model rests on marginal thinking. $45B opportunity from better pricing algorithms.

Incentives

LO 1.4: Identify how incentives shape behavior and predict unintended consequences

People Respond to Incentives

Key Concept

Incentive

Anything that motivates a person to take a particular action — rewards (positive) and penalties (negative)

Back to Uber: Surge pricing creates two incentives simultaneously

Negative incentive for riders

Higher price → some riders drop out

Positive incentive for drivers

Higher price → off-duty drivers return

One price, two incentives, both working toward balance. Elegant — but incentives don't always work this cleanly.

This is our second visit to Uber. Same story, but now we're seeing it through the incentives lens. One price change creates two incentives that push in the same direction — toward equilibrium. Transition: "But incentives don't always produce the effects we expect. Let me tell you about cobras."

The Cobra Effect

British colonial India: government offers a bounty for every dead cobra to reduce the snake population.

Phase 1: It works! People hunt cobras for the reward.

Phase 2: Entrepreneurs start breeding cobras to collect the bounty.

Phase 3: Government cancels the program. Breeders release their snakes.

Result: More cobras than before. The incentive backfired completely.

Progressive build: Phase 1 (works), Phase 2 (gaming begins), Phase 3 (backfire). Pause at each phase. Modern examples: Wells Fargo fake accounts, academic publish-or-perish. The lesson: incentives are essential, but designing them requires thinking several steps ahead.

Predict the Cobra Effect

A city has a rat problem. The government offers $10 per dead rat.
Six months later, what happens?

A

Rats decrease significantly

B

Rats stay about the same

C

Rat population increases

D

People start breeding rats

Have students commit BEFORE revealing. "Raise your hand for A... B... C... D." Most will predict A. Then reveal: "If you said D, you've predicted the cobra effect. And yes — this actually happened in Hanoi with rats." The surprise makes the concept stick.

Answer: D — People Start Breeding Rats

This actually happened in colonial Hanoi. Officials found rat farms on the city outskirts.

The cobra effect: When a bounty creates an incentive to produce the problem rather than solve it. The policy made rats valuable — so people responded rationally by breeding them.

Key insight: Incentives are essential, but designing them requires thinking several steps ahead. People respond to the incentive you create , not the one you intend .

More Incentive Surprises

Tip Fatigue

90% of Americans say tipping culture is excessive (Bankrate 2024). Average tips fell from 15.5% to 14.9%.

Tipping was designed to reward good service. When prompts appear at self-checkout , the incentive structure breaks. Consumers feel manipulated → tip less.

An incentive designed to increase income is producing resentment and lower tips.

EV Tax Credit

When the $7,500 EV tax credit was set to expire, sales spiked . After it ended, EV market share dropped from 11.8% to below 6%.

The credit didn't create permanent demand — it shifted demand forward in time . Consumers who would have bought in 2027 bought in 2025 instead.

Intertemporal substitution — changing when people act, not just whether . (Ch5, Ch8)

Two more incentive stories. Tip fatigue: the incentive to reward service backfires when it's everywhere — even self-checkout. EV tax credit: a classic intertemporal substitution — people shifted purchases forward in time, not permanently. Both show that incentives change behavior in ways designers don't always anticipate.

Incentives Meet Psychology

Organ Donation

Germany uses opt-in (you must register). Austria uses opt-out (everyone's a donor unless they decline). Austria's rate far exceeds Germany's.

In 2022, Germany expanded opt-in with awareness campaigns. Result: living donations fell 29% . The campaigns made people more anxious , not more willing.

Intended incentive (awareness) → unintended response (fear).

Buy Now, Pay Later

BNPL makes future costs feel psychologically distant. Research shows simply offering BNPL at checkout increases impulsive purchases.

The incentive to consume now — with payments deferred — overrides the incentive to save.

Another intertemporal distortion. We'll revisit behavioral biases in Ch18.

Organ donation shows how defaults shape behavior — opt-out vastly outperforms opt-in. Germany's awareness campaign backfired by triggering anxiety. BNPL shows how making future costs invisible changes present behavior. Both connect incentive design to psychology.

Behavioral Lens: The Sunk Cost Fallacy

You paid $50 for a concert ticket. On the night of the show, you feel sick. You'd rather stay home. But you go anyway: "I already paid for it — I can't let that money go to waste."

Sunk cost

A cost already incurred that cannot be recovered . The $50 is gone whether you attend or not.

The rational move

Only look forward : will you enjoy the concert more than staying home? Past payment is irrelevant.

Firms throw good money after bad on failing projects. Governments fund ineffective programs because "we've invested too much to stop." Learning to ignore sunk costs is one of the most useful lessons in economics. (Ch18)

This connects to marginal thinking — the rational decision depends only on future costs and benefits, not past spending. Students love this example because they ALL do it. Firms and governments do it too. We'll formalize this in Ch18 behavioral economics.

Trade-offs

LO 1.5: Efficiency vs. equity — the deepest trade-off in economics

The Deepest Trade-off

Efficiency

Getting the most from scarce resources — making the pie as big as possible

Equity

Distributing resources fairly — making sure everyone gets a fair slice

The tension: Policies that maximize the pie may leave some with tiny slices. Policies that equalize slices may shrink the pie . Neither is automatically better — choosing requires values, not just math.

This is exposure level only — we go deep in Chapters 6 and 7. The key message: efficiency and equity often pull in opposite directions. Neither side wins automatically. Economics clarifies the trade-offs; choosing between them is a value judgment.

The Pizza Problem

10 friends chip in $5 each for pizza. Only 6 slices arrive. How do you split it?

Efficient approach

Give pizza to the hungriest — maximizes total satisfaction

Problem: Not everyone gets any. Unequal portions.

Equitable approach

Cut into tiny pieces — everyone gets some pizza

Problem: Nobody is satisfied. Pieces too small.

This IS the efficiency-equity trade-off. More of one means less of the other. There's no perfect answer — only different trade-offs.

Relatable example before the policy-level cases. Everyone's been in this situation. The efficient solution maximizes total satisfaction but is unfair. The equitable solution is fair but leaves nobody full. There's no perfect answer — only trade-offs.

Rent Control: A Natural Experiment

Nov 2021: St. Paul voters approve a strict 3% rent cap . Minneapolis — just across the river — has no cap .

Equity win: Rents grew only 0.7% for current tenants

Efficiency cost: Building permits dropped 79% . Developers fled to Minneapolis.

This is the textbook efficiency-equity trade-off. St. Paul helped current tenants (equity) but crushed new construction (efficiency). Minneapolis serves as a natural control group — same market, different policy. We'll study rent control in depth in Chapter 7. Key takeaway: trade-offs are inescapable.

122

-3.2pp

Equity gains: Improvements in food security, housing stability, and mental health

Efficiency cost: Some workers chose to work less. Is that a problem? Depends on your values.

Reasonable people disagree — and the disagreement is about values , not facts.

122 pilots, hard data on both sides. Recipients better off on food/housing/health (equity). But employment fell 3.2pp (efficiency cost). Is the trade-off worth it? That's a normative question — economics clarifies the costs, but choosing is about values.

Trade-offs: Policy and Personal

Student Loan Forgiveness

$1.77T

total U.S. student debt

$38K

average borrower balance

Equity: Disproportionately helps lower-income and minority borrowers (Black grads owe ~88% more)

Efficiency: Each $1 of forgiveness generates only $0.02–$0.27 in economic activity

The Baby Shark Trade-off

Parent + cranky toddler at a restaurant. Hand them a phone with Baby Shark.

MB: 20 minutes of peace (immediate, tangible)

MC: More screen time, less social interaction (diffuse, long-term)

Short-run vs. long-run. Every parent, student, and worker faces this daily. Economics helps you see the costs that are easy to overlook.

Student loans: pure efficiency-equity tension with hard numbers on both sides. The debate is about values. Baby Shark: trade-offs aren't just policy — they're deeply personal. Short-run gratification vs long-run well-being. Do you scroll 10 more minutes or go to bed? Same logic.

The Allocation Game

Form groups of 3-4. Each group picks one scenario:

Scenario A

100 flu vaccines for 200 students. Who gets vaccinated?

Scenario B

50 parking spots for 300 commuters. How do you allocate?

Scenario C

3 seminar seats for 20 waitlisted students. Who gets in?

1. Choose an allocation method  |  2. Name 2 winners and 2 losers  |  3. Is your method more efficient or more equitable?

4 min group work → 2 min debrief

Let groups self-select or assign scenarios. Common methods: first-come-first-served, lottery, price, merit, need-based. During debrief, call on one group per scenario. Key insight: "Every method involves a trade-off. There's no perfect allocation method — only different trade-offs. That's the most important lesson of Chapter 1."
