Section 1

Price Elasticity of Demand

Measuring how much consumers respond

Price Elasticity of Demand

$$E_d = \left|\frac{\%\;\Delta\; Q_d}{\%\;\Delta\; P}\right|$$

Absolute value — always positive (law of demand guarantees negative ratio)

Eggs: 10% price ↑ → 1% quantity ↓

E d = 0.1 — inelastic

Streaming: 10% price ↑ → 15% subscribers ↓

E d = 1.5 — elastic

Key intuition: elasticity puts the RESPONSE (quantity) on top and the CAUSE (price) on the bottom. It's a ratio of percentages, not absolute numbers — that's what makes it comparable across goods.

The Midpoint Method

Problem: $3 → $6 is +100%, but $6 → $3 is −50%. Same change, different percentages!

$$\%\;\Delta = \frac{\text{New} - \text{Old}}{\frac{\text{New} + \text{Old}}{2}} \times 100$$

Use the average of the two values as the base

In Excel:

=ABS((B2-B1)/AVERAGE(B1,B2)*100) / ABS((A2-A1)/AVERAGE(A1,A2)*100)
The midpoint method is the only formula students need to memorize. Walk through the Excel formula — many students find this more intuitive than the math notation.

Worked Example: Campus Coffee

Latte price: $4.50 → $5.50  |  Daily sales: 200 → 160 cups

Step 1 — % Δ Price:   ($5.50 − $4.50) / $5.00 × 100 = 20%

Step 2 — % Δ Quantity:   (160 − 200) / 180 × 100 = −22.2%

Step 3 — Elasticity:   |−22.2% / 20%| = 1.11

E d > 1 → Elastic demand — quantity responds more than proportionally

Have students attempt the calculation on paper for 30 seconds BEFORE you reveal each step. This activates Bloom's L3 (apply). The progressive reveal keeps them engaged.

Section 2

Elastic or Inelastic?

Classifying and explaining consumer responsiveness

The Elasticity Spectrum

▮ Inelastic (E d < 1)    ▮ Unit elastic    ▮ Elastic (E d > 1)

This is the anchor visual for the lecture. Keep referring back to it. "Where does this good fall on our spectrum?" Goods at the top have few substitutes and are necessities. Goods at the bottom have abundant substitutes and are luxuries.

What Determines Elasticity?

 Close Substitutes

Most important factor. McDonald’s → elastic (Chipotle, Wendy’s). Eggs → inelastic (no replacement).

 Necessity vs. Luxury

Necessities (gasoline, insulin) → inelastic. Luxuries (dining out, travel) → elastic.

 Market Definition

Narrow → elastic (“Starbucks latte”). Broad → inelastic (“beverages”).

 Time Horizon

Short run → inelastic (can’t adjust). Long run → elastic (buy EV, move, change habits).

Ask students: "Which of these four factors is the most important?" (Answer: substitutes.) Then: "Can you see how all four explain why eggs are inelastic?" This leads directly into Discussion 1.

Common Misconception

 WRONG

“Elasticity and slope are the same thing.”

 RIGHT

Slope = ratio of absolute changes (fixed).
Elasticity = ratio of percentage changes (varies along the curve).

Why it matters: On a straight-line demand curve, demand is elastic at high prices (top) and inelastic at low prices (bottom). Firms need to know where they are on the curve.

This is one of the hardest distinctions for students. Draw a linear demand curve and mark the midpoint — above is elastic, below is inelastic. Same slope everywhere, but elasticity changes!

Section 3

Elasticity & Total Revenue

The billion-dollar question: raise the price or not?

The Total Revenue Test

TR = P × Q

Two competing forces when price rises:

Price Effect

Higher price per unit → revenue up

Quantity Effect

Fewer units sold → revenue down

Demand Price ↑ Price ↓ Winner
Elastic (E d > 1) TR falls TR rises Quantity effect
Unit elastic TR unchanged TR unchanged Tie
Inelastic (E d < 1) TR rises TR falls Price effect
This is the core business insight. Draw the P×Q rectangle on the board. When price rises, the rectangle gets taller but narrower. Whether area grows or shrinks depends on elasticity.

Total Revenue: Two Stories

Campus Coffee (Elastic, E d = 1.11)

Before: $4.50 × 200 = $900/day

After: $5.50 × 160 = $880/day

TR fell $20

Quantity effect won. Bad pricing decision.

Eggs (Inelastic, E d = 0.03)

Before: $2.50 × 100M = $250M

After: $5.00 × 98M = $490M

TR nearly doubled

Price effect won. Supply shock increased revenue.

This is the key contrast. Same logic, opposite outcomes. Campus Coffee lost revenue by raising prices (elastic). Egg farmers gained revenue from the supply shock (inelastic). This is why elasticity matters for business strategy.

Netflix: A Total Revenue Masterclass

Inelastic Segment

Standard plan: $15.49 → $18.99

Result: +50M subscribers

Revenue: $34 billion

Netflix became a household necessity. Price hikes raised TR.

Elastic Segment

Ad tier: $7.99/month

Captured: 94M users

Monetizes via: advertising

Price-sensitive consumers get a lower price. Netflix keeps them.

Strategy: Charge more to inelastic consumers (ad-free). Retain elastic consumers at a lower price (ads). Maximize total revenue from both segments.

This is tiered pricing based on elasticity segmentation. Students love this example because they all use Netflix. Ask: "Which tier are you on? What does that say about YOUR elasticity?"

The Farming Paradox

Why can good news for farmers be bad news for farmers?

 New wheat variety boosts yields 20% → supply shifts right

 Demand for wheat is inelastic — people don’t eat much more bread when it’s cheaper

 Price drop (%) exceeds quantity increase (%) → TR falls

 This is why farmers historically receive government subsidies

This counterintuitive result confuses students every year. The key: with inelastic demand, increasing supply makes prices fall MORE than quantity rises. Total revenue shrinks despite producing more.

Section 4

Cross-Price & Income Elasticity

How demand responds to other prices and income

Cross-Price Elasticity

$$E_{cross} = \frac{\%\;\Delta\; Q_A}{\%\;\Delta\; P_B}$$

The sign tells you the relationship

+

Substitutes

Netflix ↑ → Disney+ ↑

Cigarettes ↑ → E-cigs ↑

−

Complements

Phones ↑ → Cases ↓

Gas ↑ → SUVs ↓

0

Unrelated

Eggs ↑ → Lawn mowers? No effect.

Streaming churn illustrates cross-price substitution: 42% of subscribers do "serial churning" — cancel one, subscribe to another. Bundles (Disney+/Hulu/ESPN+) reduce effective cross-price elasticity by raising switching costs.

Income Elasticity

$$E_{income} = \frac{\%\;\Delta\; Q_d}{\%\;\Delta\; Income}$$

Necessity

0 < E < 1

Groceries, gasoline, basic clothing

Income ×2 ≠ bread ×2

Luxury

E > 1

Hermès, foreign travel, premium streaming

Hermès grew 15% in 2024

Inferior Good

E < 0

Store-brand groceries, instant noodles, bus tickets

Private labels surged to $271B

“Inferior” is a technical term about income-demand relationship, not a quality judgment.

Key distinction: "inferior" doesn't mean "bad" — it means demand moves opposite to income. Students resist this terminology. Emphasize the 2024-25 trading-down pattern: inflation eroding real income → store brands surging.

Section 5

Supply Elasticity & Tax Incidence

Who really pays when the government taxes a good?

Supply Elasticity: Time Is Everything

Market period: Supply fixed. Flower shop on Valentine’s Day.

Short run: Extra shifts, spare capacity. Egg farms raise replacement pullets.

Long run: New firms enter. Austin adds 120K housing units.

Austin (elastic supply): rents fell 16%. SF (inelastic): prices soared.

Austin vs San Francisco is the best supply elasticity comparison. Austin reformed zoning → elastic supply → more homes, lower rents. SF restricted building → inelastic supply → all demand pressure became price increases.

Tax Incidence: The Inelastic Side Pays

The side that can’t escape the tax bears the burden.

Regardless of who legally writes the check.

Cigarettes (PED 0.3–0.4)

Smokers bear most of the tax. Can’t easily quit. Avg state tax: $2.01/pack.

Youth cigarettes (PED 0.7)

More elastic! Taxes prevent youth smoking better than reduce adult smoking.

Payroll taxes (50/50 legal split)

Labor supply inelastic → employees bear most of burden through lower wages.

The statutory 50/50 split of payroll taxes is economically irrelevant — what matters is relative elasticities. This principle applies to every tax: cigarettes, carbon, soda, tariffs.

Discussion: The Policymaker’s Paradox

You’re advising the governor on funding universal pre-K. Two options:

Option A: Cigarette Tax

+$1/pack • PED = 0.3

Option B: Soda Tax

1.5¢/oz • PED = 0.8

 Which raises more stable revenue ?

 Which changes behavior more effectively?

 Which is fairer — and how do you define fair?

5 min
3 min debate, 2 min debrief. Key insight: cigarette tax is better for revenue (inelastic base doesn't shrink), soda tax is better for behavior change (elastic response). But cigarette tax is regressive — burden falls on low-income smokers. No tax maximizes BOTH revenue AND behavior change.

Key Takeaways

1. Elasticity measures responsiveness — ratio of percentage changes, not slope.

2. Midpoint method eliminates directional asymmetry.

3. Total revenue test — elastic → price hike hurts. Inelastic → price hike helps.

4. Four determinants — substitutes, necessity, market definition, time horizon.

5. The inelastic side bears the tax burden — governs every debate about sin taxes, carbon taxes, and payroll taxes.
