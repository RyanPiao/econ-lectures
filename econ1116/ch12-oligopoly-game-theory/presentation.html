Section 1

Oligopoly

The in-between market

Let's start with the market structure itself. Oligopoly literally means "few sellers." It sits between monopoly and monopolistic competition on the spectrum. The defining feature is strategic interdependence — each firm's profit depends on what the other firms do.

Market Structure Spectrum

Figure 12.1 — Market structures vary along a spectrum defined by number of firms, product differentiation, and barriers to entry.
This figure shows the four market structures on a spectrum. On the left, perfect competition with many firms selling identical products. On the right, monopoly with one firm. In between we have monopolistic competition — many firms with differentiated products — and oligopoly — a few firms with high barriers to entry. The key insight is that oligopoly is the only structure where firms must think strategically about rivals' reactions. A perfectly competitive wheat farmer doesn't care what one other farmer does. A monopolist has no rivals. But Coca-Cola absolutely cares what Pepsi does next.

Measuring Market Concentration

CR4 — Concentration Ratio

\[ CR_4 = s_1 + s_2 + s_3 + s_4 \]

Sum of market shares of the top 4 firms.
Simple but ignores distribution within the top 4.

HHI — Herfindahl-Hirschman Index

\[ HHI = \sum_{i=1}^{N} s_i^2 \]

Sum of squared market shares (as percentages).
Ranges from ~0 (competitive) to 10,000 (monopoly).

HHI Range DOJ Classification Merger Scrutiny
< 1,500 Unconcentrated Generally approved
1,500 – 2,500 Moderately concentrated Review if ΔHHI > 100
> 2,500 Highly concentrated Presumed anticompetitive if ΔHHI > 200
Two tools for measuring concentration. CR4 is simple — add up the market shares of the four biggest firms. If CR4 is above 60%, economists typically call it an oligopoly. But CR4 has a blind spot: it treats a market with shares 25-25-25-25 the same as one with 90-4-3-3. The HHI fixes this by squaring shares, which gives disproportionate weight to larger firms. The DOJ uses HHI thresholds when reviewing mergers. Below 1,500 is generally fine. Above 2,500 raises red flags. We'll use both measures today.

HHI Across Industries

HHI values for selected US industries (2024). Red dashed line = DOJ "highly concentrated" threshold (2,500).

This chart shows HHI values for real US industries. Notice how wireless carriers and search engines are extremely concentrated — those are tight oligopolies. Restaurants and retail are on the low end. The red dashed line at 2,500 marks the DOJ threshold for "highly concentrated." When you see industries above that line, you're looking at markets where a few firms have enormous pricing power. Keep these numbers in mind — we'll come back to them when we discuss mergers.

The Kinked Demand Curve

  • Raise price: rivals don't follow → you lose customers fast (elastic demand above kink)
  • Cut price: rivals match immediately → you gain few customers (inelastic demand below kink)

Price stickiness: MC can shift within the MR gap without changing the optimal price. This explains why gas stations on the same block charge identical prices for weeks.

The kinked demand curve is one of the oldest models of oligopoly pricing. The logic is asymmetric: if you raise your price, rivals are happy to keep theirs low and steal your customers, so demand above the current price is very elastic. But if you cut your price, rivals match immediately to prevent losing share, so demand below the current price is inelastic. This creates a kink at the current price and a gap in the marginal revenue curve. Costs can shift within that gap and the profit-maximizing price stays the same. This explains the price stickiness we observe in gasoline, airlines, and many other oligopoly markets.

Section 2

Game Theory

Strategic decision-making

Now we have the tools to identify oligopoly markets. But how do firms actually behave in these markets? That's where game theory comes in. Game theory is the mathematical study of strategic interaction — situations where your best choice depends on what someone else chooses. Developed by John von Neumann and John Nash in the mid-20th century, it's now essential in economics, political science, biology, and computer science.

The Payoff Matrix

Two gas stations across the street: EZ Gas and QuikFill . Each independently sets price: High or Low .

QuikFill
High Low
EZ Gas High $800 , $800 $300 , $1,100
Low $1,100 , $300 $500 , $500

Payoffs: EZ Gas , QuikFill  |  Weekly profit in dollars  |  Nash equilibrium   Cooperative outcome

Here is our first payoff matrix. Read it this way: EZ Gas chooses a row, QuikFill chooses a column. The cell where they intersect shows both payoffs — EZ Gas first in blue, QuikFill second in terracotta. If both set high prices, they each earn $800 per week. If both go low, they each earn only $500. But if one goes low while the other stays high, the low-price station steals customers and earns $1,100 while the high-price station earns only $300. The green-shaded cell is the cooperative outcome — both would prefer it. The terracotta-bordered cell is the Nash equilibrium — where they actually end up. Let's see why.

Finding the Dominant Strategy

Think like EZ Gas...

  • If QuikFill chooses High:
    EZ Gas High → $800  vs.  EZ Gas Low → $1,100   Low is better
  • If QuikFill chooses Low:
    EZ Gas High → $300  vs.  EZ Gas Low → $500   Low is better

Low is EZ Gas's dominant strategy — it's the best response regardless of what QuikFill does. By symmetry, Low is also QuikFill's dominant strategy.

A dominant strategy is one that yields a higher payoff than any alternative, no matter what the opponent does.

Let's walk through EZ Gas's decision. Suppose QuikFill chooses High. Then EZ Gas gets $800 from High or $1,100 from Low — Low is better. Now suppose QuikFill chooses Low. Then EZ Gas gets $300 from High or $500 from Low — Low is still better. No matter what QuikFill does, EZ Gas prefers Low. That makes Low a dominant strategy. QuikFill faces the exact same logic by symmetry. So both choose Low, earning $500 each — even though both choosing High would give them $800 each. This is the prisoner's dilemma in action.

The Prisoner's Dilemma

Each player has a dominant strategy (defect), but mutual defection makes both worse off than mutual cooperation.

Individual rationality leads to collective irrationality. This is the core tension of oligopoly.

Named after a 1950 thought experiment by Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher at RAND Corporation.

The prisoner's dilemma is one of the most important ideas in social science. The original story involves two suspects arrested for a crime. If both stay silent (cooperate), they get light sentences. If both confess (defect), they get heavy sentences. But if one confesses while the other stays silent, the confessor goes free while the silent one gets the harshest penalty. The Nash equilibrium is both confess — even though both staying silent would be better for both. This exact structure appears everywhere in economics: price wars, advertising races, arms races, environmental degradation, overfishing. The gas station example we just analyzed is a prisoner's dilemma.

Coke vs. Pepsi: The Ad Arms Race

Pepsi

Coke Advertise $3B , $2B $5B , $1B

Payoffs = annual operating profit (stylized). Nash   Cooperative

~$4B in joint profit destroyed annually by the advertising arms race. Neither can stop because stopping unilaterally means losing share.

This answers our opening question.

Now we can answer the puzzle from the opening slide. Coke and Pepsi are trapped in a prisoner's dilemma. If both advertise, they earn $3B and $2B respectively — the Nash equilibrium. If neither advertised, they'd earn $5B and $3.5B — the cooperative outcome. But neither can unilaterally stop advertising because the other would steal their market share. The result: about $4 billion in combined profit is destroyed every year on advertising that largely cancels out. This is not irrational behavior by either firm — each is making its best individual choice. It's the structure of the game that produces the collectively bad outcome.

Game of Chicken: SAG-AFTRA Strike

Studios
Hold Out Concede
SAG-AFTRA Hold Out -$5B, -$5B +$2B , -$1B
Concede -$1B , +$2B $0 , $0
  • No dominant strategy — best response depends on opponent's choice
  • Two Nash equilibria: (Hold Out, Concede) and (Concede, Hold Out)

Analysis

"Can I credibly convince my opponent I will never swerve?" How did SAG-AFTRA's 118-day strike signal commitment?

Not every game is a prisoner's dilemma. The game of chicken has a different structure: the worst outcome is mutual confrontation (both hold out — the crash). There's no dominant strategy here — your best move depends on what the other side does. If the studios hold out, you want to concede. If they concede, you want to hold out. There are two Nash equilibria, and the question becomes: who blinks first? In the 2023 SAG-AFTRA strike, both sides tried to signal they wouldn't blink — by burning money publicly. The strike lasted 118 days because credible commitment was the key strategic variable. This is a different game with different strategic logic than the prisoner's dilemma.

Section 3

Nash Equilibrium

Stability does not mean optimality

We've been using the term Nash equilibrium informally. Let's now define it precisely and learn how to find it systematically. Named after John Nash, whose work earned him the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics. The key lesson of this section: Nash equilibrium tells us where the game settles, but that resting point may leave everyone worse off than an alternative outcome.

Finding Nash Equilibria

Best-Response Method

  1. For each of Player 1's strategies, find Player 2's best response → underline that payoff
  2. For each of Player 2's strategies, find Player 1's best response → underline that payoff
  3. Any cell with both payoffs underlined is a Nash equilibrium

Nash equilibrium: no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing strategy.

Here's the systematic method for finding Nash equilibria. Step one: go column by column and ask "if Player 2 plays this column, what's Player 1's best response?" Underline that payoff. Step two: go row by row and ask "if Player 1 plays this row, what's Player 2's best response?" Underline that payoff. Step three: any cell where both payoffs are underlined is a Nash equilibrium — neither player wants to deviate. In the gas station game, (Low, Low) has both payoffs underlined. In the chicken game, (Hold Out, Concede) and (Concede, Hold Out) both have both payoffs underlined. Practice this method — it works for any 2x2 game.

Common Misconception

WRONG: "Nash equilibrium means everyone is happy with the outcome."

RIGHT: Nash equilibrium means no one can unilaterally improve. Both gas stations earn $500 at Nash but would prefer $800 at the cooperative outcome. Stable ≠ optimal.

Real-world examples of suboptimal Nash equilibria:

  • Traffic: Everyone drives → congestion. Each driver is best-responding, yet all would prefer less traffic.
  • Arms races: Both countries stockpile weapons → neither is safer, both are poorer.
  • Overfishing: Each boat maximizes catch → fish stocks collapse for everyone.
This is the single most common mistake students make on exams. Nash equilibrium does NOT mean the outcome is good, efficient, or preferred. It only means no single player can do better by changing their own strategy alone. In the prisoner's dilemma, the Nash equilibrium is the worst possible joint outcome. Both players would prefer to cooperate, but neither can enforce cooperation unilaterally. The examples on screen — traffic congestion, arms races, overfishing — are all Nash equilibria that make everyone worse off. This gap between individual rationality and collective welfare is at the heart of many policy debates.

Section 4

Cartels & Collusion

Why cooperation keeps falling apart

If the prisoner's dilemma makes firms worse off, why not just agree to cooperate? That's exactly what cartels try to do. A cartel is a group of firms that explicitly coordinate production or pricing to maximize joint profits. But as we'll see, the prisoner's dilemma logic makes cartels inherently unstable. Every member has an incentive to cheat.

OPEC+: The World's Most Famous Cartel

Saudi Arabia Cut Output High price

  • 2025–2026: 3.6M barrels/day in voluntary cuts maintained through end of 2026
  • Russia: consistently overproducing via "condensate" classification loopholes
  • Result: Brent crude volatile between $65–85 as compliance wavers
OPEC+ is the world's largest and most visible cartel. The payoff matrix shows the classic structure: if all members cut output, oil prices stay high and everyone profits. But each individual member can secretly overproduce to grab extra revenue at the cartel price. Russia has been doing exactly this — classifying excess output as "condensate" rather than crude oil to technically comply while actually overproducing. As of early 2026, OPEC+ has maintained 3.6 million barrels per day in cuts, but compliance is spotty. The cartel survives because Saudi Arabia acts as the swing producer — absorbing the cost of other members' cheating. But there's a limit to Saudi patience.

Why Cartels Break Down

Cheating is structural

Every member's dominant strategy is to overproduce while others cut. The prisoner's dilemma is baked in.

Detection is slow

Output data is reported with lags. By the time cheating is detected, damage is done.

More members = more cracks

OPEC+ has 23 members. Coordination costs rise exponentially with group size.

Outsiders fill the gap

US shale, Guyana, Brazil expand when cartel pushes prices up — undermining the whole effort.

Empirical record: most cartels collapse within 5–8 years. OPEC has survived since 1960 only because Saudi Arabia absorbs the cost of others' cheating.

Four structural reasons cartels fail. First, cheating isn't a bug — it's a feature of the game's structure. Every member's dominant strategy is to defect. Second, monitoring is imperfect. OPEC members self-report production data, and independent verification takes months. Third, more members means more potential defectors. OPEC+ has 23 members — coordinating that many sovereign nations is extraordinarily difficult. Fourth, high cartel prices attract outside competition. US shale producers ramp up whenever oil prices rise above $70, effectively putting a ceiling on OPEC's market power. The historical data confirms this: most cartels last 5 to 8 years before collapsing. OPEC is the exception, not the rule.

Algorithmic Collusion

When machines learn to cooperate

  • RealPage (Nov 2025): DOJ settlement over rental pricing algorithm used by landlords managing 4M+ units. Algorithm recommended identical rent increases.
  • Amazon "Project Nessie" (FTC trial, Oct 2026): Internal algorithm that tested raising prices, watched if competitors followed, then kept higher prices if they did.
  • California SB 1790 (Jan 2026): First US state law explicitly banning algorithmic price-fixing in housing.

Evaluation (L6)

"If algorithms produce cartel-like prices without any human communication, is that collusion? Should it be illegal?"

Here's where the cartel story gets modern and genuinely unsettled. RealPage provided software to landlords that ingested competitors' rent data and recommended prices. The result was coordinated rent increases across millions of apartments — without any landlord ever calling another landlord. The DOJ argued this was algorithmic collusion. Amazon's Project Nessie was even more explicit — an internal algorithm designed to raise prices and see if competitors followed. California became the first state to ban algorithmic price coordination in housing. The legal question is fascinating: traditional antitrust law requires an "agreement" between competitors. But what if the algorithm coordinates prices without any explicit agreement? This is one of the biggest open questions in antitrust law right now. Let students debate this for 2-3 minutes.

Section 5

Monopolistic Competition

The cost of variety

Let's shift to the other "in between" market structure: monopolistic competition. This is the most common market structure in everyday life — restaurants, clothing stores, hair salons, apps. Many firms, differentiated products, low barriers to entry. Each firm has a tiny bit of market power because its product is slightly different, but competition drives profits to zero in the long run.

Monopolistic Competition: SR vs. LR

Short run: Economic profit possible. Firm faces downward-sloping demand (differentiated product) and sets P > MC.

Long run: Entry eliminates profit. Demand shifts left until P = ATC. Firm operates with excess capacity .

Figure 12.6 — The long-run zero-profit condition tangency: demand is tangent to ATC at the profit-maximizing quantity.
The figure shows both panels. In the short run, a monopolistically competitive firm can earn economic profit — the shaded rectangle between price and average total cost. But because barriers to entry are low, new firms enter the market attracted by those profits. Entry shifts the demand curve for each existing firm to the left — each firm gets a smaller slice of the market. This continues until demand is just tangent to ATC, meaning price equals average total cost and economic profit is zero. Notice the firm produces to the left of the ATC minimum — this is excess capacity. Society pays for variety through slightly higher prices and underutilized capacity. Think of all the half-empty restaurants on your street — that's excess capacity in monopolistic competition.

Poll: Merger Math

A market has 4 equal-sized firms , each with 25% market share. Two of them propose a merger.

Before Merger

\[ HHI = 4 \times 25^2 = 2{,}500 \]

Right at the "highly concentrated" threshold

After Merger

\[ HHI = 50^2 + 25^2 + 25^2 = 3{,}750 \]

\(\Delta HHI = 1{,}250\) — far exceeds the DOJ threshold of 200

YES — HHI > 2,500 and ΔHHI > 200

NO — Market was already concentrated

Rule of thumb: \(\Delta HHI = 2 \times s_1 \times s_2\). Merging two 25% firms: \(2 \times 25 \times 25 = 1{,}250\). Quick way to estimate merger impact.

Let's practice HHI math. Before the merger, four firms each with 25% share gives HHI of 2,500 — right at the DOJ threshold. After two firms merge, we have a 50% firm and two 25% firms. New HHI is 2,500 + 625 + 625 = 3,750. The change is 1,250, which massively exceeds the DOJ's threshold of 200 for presumptively anticompetitive mergers. There's a neat shortcut: the change in HHI from a merger always equals 2 times the product of the merging firms' shares. This is useful for back-of-envelope calculations. The DOJ would almost certainly challenge this merger unless the firms could demonstrate extraordinary efficiency gains.

Section 6

Repeated Games

How cooperation survives

So far we've analyzed one-shot games where firms interact once. But in reality, Coke and Pepsi compete every single day, forever. OPEC members meet quarterly. Your local gas stations are across the street from each other permanently. When games are repeated, the strategic calculus changes dramatically. Cooperation becomes possible — not guaranteed, but possible — because firms can punish cheaters in future rounds.

Cooperation Through Repetition

Trigger Strategies

  • Tit-for-Tat: Start by cooperating. Then copy whatever your opponent did last round.
  • Grim Trigger: Cooperate until betrayed. Then defect forever .

TSMC & Apple: Mutual lock-in sustains cooperation. Apple buys ~25% of TSMC's output; TSMC builds fabs specifically for Apple chips. Neither can profitably defect.

Folk Theorem: When firms interact indefinitely and value the future sufficiently, any mutually beneficial outcome can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium through trigger strategies.

Cooperation is sustainable when:

\(\text{Future cooperation value} > \text{One-time defection gain}\)

Higher discount factor (patient firms), frequent interaction, easy monitoring, and few players all help.

The folk theorem is one of the most powerful results in game theory. It says that in infinitely repeated games, if players are patient enough, cooperation can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium. The mechanism is punishment: if you cheat today, I punish you in all future periods. The two main punishment strategies are tit-for-tat — which is forgiving and mirrors your opponent — and grim trigger — which is harsh and permanent. Tit-for-tat actually won Robert Axelrod's famous computer tournament in the 1980s. The TSMC-Apple example shows how real firms create cooperation through mutual dependence. Apple can't easily switch chip manufacturers, and TSMC can't easily replace Apple's orders. This mutual lock-in functions like a repeated game with very high switching costs — making defection extremely costly.

Key Takeaways

Oligopoly = strategic interdependence. CR4 and HHI measure concentration; the kinked demand curve explains price stickiness.

Payoff matrices + Nash equilibrium. Find dominant strategies first; Nash = no unilateral improvement. Stable ≠ optimal.

Cartels are prisoner's dilemmas. Cheating is each member's dominant strategy, so cartels tend to collapse within 5–8 years.

Repeated games change everything. Trigger strategies sustain cooperation — but only if the game has no known endpoint.

Monopolistic competition: zero long-run profit with excess capacity — the price society pays for variety.

Six takeaways that map directly to the learning objectives. First, oligopoly is defined by strategic interdependence — use CR4 and HHI to identify it. Second, payoff matrices are the tool for organizing strategic thinking. Third, Nash equilibrium is about stability, not optimality — the most testable concept in this chapter. Fourth, cartels are unstable because of the prisoner's dilemma structure. Fifth, monopolistic competition looks like monopoly in the short run but competition wins in the long run through entry. Sixth, repeated games change everything — cooperation becomes possible when the future matters enough. These six points cover everything you need for the exam.
