Section 1

Markets, Prices & Demand

LO 4.1 — Draw demand curves and read them

What Is a Market?

Market — any arrangement bringing buyers and sellers together to exchange goods, services, or resources.

Competitive market — many buyers, many sellers, no one influences the price. Both sides are price takers.

Prices as Signals

When eggs became scarce, the price told consumers "find alternatives" and told farmers "rebuild your flocks NOW."

No committee met. The price mechanism did the work.

Uber surge pricing does this in real time — concert ends, prices triple, more drivers appear within minutes.

"Think of prices as the economy's nervous system. Scarce? Price rises. Abundant? Price falls. No central planner needed."

Law of Demand: All else equal, as price ↑, quantity demanded ↓.

Two forces:

  • Substitution effect: Avocados expensive? Switch to hummus.
  • Income effect: Avocados expensive? Budget doesn't stretch — buy fewer.
"Notice the pattern: price goes up, quantity goes down. Every row. This is the law of demand — one of the most reliable relationships in economics."

The Demand Curve

Every point answers: "At this price, how much will buyers want?"

"P on the vertical axis, Q on the horizontal — Marshall convention from the 1890s. Pick a price, trace to the curve, drop to Q. That's how you read it."

Poll

The price of avocados drops from $2.00 to $1.50. Consumers buy more avocados. This is:

A. An increase in demand
B. An increase in quantity demanded
C. A decrease in supply
D. A decrease in quantity demanded

Answer: B. The price of avocados itself changed — we moved along the curve to a new point. The curve didn't shift. This distinction is everything.

"If many chose A, pause: 'What would have to change for it to be an increase in demand? Not the price of avocados — something else.'"

Section 2

Shifts in Demand

LO 4.2 & 4.3 — What moves the curve vs. moving along it

5 Demand Shifters

1. Income

Normal goods: income ↑ → demand ↑
Inferior goods: income ↑ → demand ↓

2. Related Goods

Substitutes: Uber surges → Lyft demand ↑
Complements: Gas ↑ → SUV demand ↓

3. Tastes

TikTok trend → demand surges overnight. Avocado toast tripled U.S. consumption.

4. Expectations

Expect prices to rise → buy more now. Expect recession → pull back spending.

5. Number of Buyers

Population growth, immigration, new market access → demand shifts right.

"These are the 5 demand shifters. Any one of them changes — the WHOLE curve moves. The price of the good itself? That's a movement ALONG the curve."

Which demand shifters? Tastes (more time at home), expectations (lockdowns last months), income (stimulus checks).

"Prices didn't fall. People subscribed because SOMETHING ELSE changed — their tastes, expectations, income. That's a demand shift, not a movement along."

The #1 Exam Mistake

Movement Along

The price of this good changed.

Curve stays put. You move to a new point on the same curve.

Shift of the Curve

Something other than the price changed.

The entire curve moves right or left.

The test: "Did the price of this good change?" → Movement along   |   "Did something else change?" → Curve shifts

Misconception: "An increase in demand means more people bought it."

Not necessarily. An increase in demand means the curve shifted right — buyers want more at every price.

"This is the #1 mistake on every economics exam. We're testing it three times today — in Poll 1, this discussion, and Poll 2. If you master this, you're ahead of most students."

Law of Supply: All else equal, as price ↑, quantity supplied ↑.

Why? Higher prices make production more profitable → existing sellers produce more, new sellers enter.

The symmetry: Demand slopes ⬇ down (higher prices discourage buying). Supply slopes ⬆ up (higher prices encourage selling).

"Notice the opposite pattern from demand: price up, quantity up. These opposing forces are what make markets work."

The Supply Curve

Every point answers: "At this price, how much will sellers offer?"

"Upward-sloping — the mirror image of demand. Both curves together give us the market."

6 Supply Shifters

1. Input Prices

Costs ↑ → supply shifts left

2. Technology

Better tech → supply shifts right

3. Expectations

Expect P ↑ → hold back now

4. # of Sellers

More sellers → supply shifts right

5. Gov't Policy

Tax → left; Subsidy → right

6. Related Production

Lime prices ↑ → avocado supply ↓

Same rule applies: Price of this good changes → movement along. Anything else → curve shifts.

"6 shifters for supply, 5 for demand. Master these lists and you can analyze any market."

Discussion: Price Gouging

5 min — Open Debate

Bloom's L5: Evaluate

After a hurricane, bottled water and generators spike in price. Some states cap prices with "anti-price-gouging" laws.

Using the S&D model: what happens to the quantity available if prices are capped below equilibrium? Who benefits? Who is harmed?

Pro-cap: Low-income families can't afford $10 water; spikes feel exploitative

Anti-cap: Capped prices → no incentive for suppliers to rush in → shortages persist

"This is where economics meets ethics. The model shows capping prices creates shortages. But is that the whole story? We'll formalize this in Topic 7 with price ceiling diagrams."

Debrief: The Price Mechanism

When water prices spike, the price signal simultaneously tells consumers "conserve" and suppliers "rush more in."

Cap the price, and you mute that signal. The market loses its ability to self-correct.

Topic 7 preview: We'll draw price ceiling diagrams and measure the welfare consequences — deadweight loss, misallocation, and black markets.

"Whether we SHOULD let prices rise in emergencies is normative. How the mechanism WORKS is positive — and that's what this chapter equips you to analyze."

Key Takeaways

1. Markets coordinate through prices. Scarce → price ↑ → rations demand, incentivizes supply.

2. Demand ↓, Supply ↑. Law of demand + law of supply create the tension that makes markets work.

3. Shift vs. movement is everything. Price changes → move along. Everything else → curve shifts.

4. Equilibrium = where curves cross. Surpluses push P ↓, shortages push P ↑. Markets self-correct.

5. Comparative statics is your prediction tool. Identify the shift, trace the new equilibrium. When both curves shift, one variable is ambiguous.

"Five sentences that capture the whole chapter. If you remember nothing else, remember #3 — shift vs. movement is the key."
