Section 1

The Demand for Labor

From product markets to labor markets — firms as buyers

Derived Demand & MRP

The Big Flip

In product markets, firms are sellers .

In labor markets, firms are buyers — buying your time, skills, and effort.

Labor demand is a derived demand : it depends on the demand for the product labor produces.

Starbucks hires baristas because customers want lattes — not for the joy of having employees.

Marginal Revenue Product

MRP = MP × P

MP = additional output from one more worker

P = price of the output

MRP = what that worker is worth to you in dollars

Hiring rule: Hire until W = MRP

Worked Example: Priya's Tutoring Center

Priya charges $40/session and pays tutors $200/day .

Tutors Sessions/Day MP MRP (MP × $40) Hire?
1 8 8 $320 Yes
2 14 6 $240 Yes
3 18 4 $160 No
4 20 2 $80 No

Result: Priya hires 2 tutors . Diminishing marginal returns → each worker adds less → MRP curve slopes down → this IS the labor demand curve.

The MRP Curve = The Labor Demand Curve

The downward slope reflects diminishing marginal returns : each additional worker adds less revenue.

Section 2

Why Wages Differ

Compensating differentials, human capital, and efficiency wages

Compensating Differentials

Some jobs are dangerous, unpleasant, or inconvenient. Workers demand higher pay to compensate.

Alaskan Crab Fisher

$50,000+ / few months

One of the deadliest jobs

Night-Shift Nurse

+10–15% premium

Inconvenient hours

Remote Worker

–10–15% pay cut

Desirable perk priced in

Human Capital

The most powerful predictor of wages is human capital — education, training, skills, and experience.

  • Bachelor's degree holders earn 67% more per week than high school grads

  • Lifetime college premium: ~$1.2 million

  • But: coding bootcamps ($14K, 12–28 weeks) → avg starting salary $70,698

Education raises your marginal product , which raises your MRP , which raises your wage .

Efficiency Wages: When Paying More Costs Less

Henry Ford, 1914

Doubled wages to $5/day

Annual turnover was 300% — hired 52,000/year to fill 14,000 slots

Result: Turnover plunged, productivity soared, unit costs fell

Costco vs. Walmart, Today

Costco pays above-market wages + benefits

Employee turnover: 6%

Walmart turnover: 44%

Same logic, 100+ years later.

Efficiency wages = paying above-market to reduce turnover, attract better workers, and boost effort. The higher wage pays for itself .

Section 3

Discrimination & Market Power

When equally productive workers face unequal treatment

Labor Market Discrimination

The Wage Gaps (2024)

  • Women: 84¢ per male dollar

  • Black men: 79¢

  • Hispanic men: 73¢

Two Types

Taste-based: Employer prefers one group over another (Becker, 1957)

Statistical: Employer uses group averages as proxy for individual quality

The Resume Audit Study

Bertrand & Mullainathan sent identical resumes to employers

Only difference: the name at the top

"White" names got 50% more callbacks

Same qualifications. Same experience. Different treatment.

Common Misconception

"The 84¢ figure means women are paid less for the exact same job."

What the data actually shows

84¢ compares all full-time women to all full-time men

Controlling for occupation, hours, experience: gap narrows to 5–8¢

But that's not the whole story

Claudia Goldin (2023 Nobel): the gap comes from "greedy work"

Jobs rewarding long, inflexible hours pay disproportionately more

Women still bear more caregiving → less access to these roles

The question is not just "equal pay for equal work" but "why are the most lucrative jobs designed to exclude many women?"

Monopsony: When Employers Have Market Power

A monopsony is a market with a single (or dominant) buyer of labor.

  • Classic: coal company in small Appalachian town

  • Modern: only hospital within 50 miles

The monopsonist can push wages below competitive levels because workers have few alternatives.

Non-Compete Clauses

By 2024, 18% of US workers bound by non-competes

Including fast-food and warehouse workers

FTC estimate: eliminating non-competes would raise wages by $300 billion/year

Section 4

Measuring Inequality

Lorenz curves, Gini coefficients, and what's driving the trend

The Lorenz Curve

Line up every household from poorest to richest .

  • 45° line = perfect equality

  • US: Bottom 20% earn ~3%; top 20% earn ~52%

The bigger the bow, the more unequal the distribution.

The Gini Coefficient

Gini = A / (A + B)

A = area between equality line and Lorenz curve

B = area under the Lorenz curve

0 = perfect equality    1 = perfect inequality

Think of it as: what fraction of the "inequality triangle" is actually unequal?

5-Person Example

Incomes: $10, $20, $30, $40, $100

Total = $200

Bottom 20% 5%
Bottom 40% 15%
Bottom 60% 30%
Bottom 80% 50%
100% 100%

Gini ≈ 0.36

What's Driving Rising Inequality?

1940s–70s: "Great Compression" — inequality fell

Since 1980: Top 1% share: 8% → ~20%

CEO pay ratio: 20:1 → 280:1

Four forces: Tech, globalization, unions ↓, policy

Section 5

AI & the Future of Work

Task model, job polarization, and what it means for you

AI Automates Tasks, Not Jobs

Autor's Task Model

Technology replaces routine tasks (cognitive + manual)

It complements nonroutine tasks: creativity, judgment, empathy

Result: job polarization — middle hollowed out, growth at top and bottom

Klarna: A Cautionary Tale

Swedish fintech cut staff 5,500 → 3,400, claimed AI = 700 agents

By late 2025: service quality crashed. Had to rehire humans.

AI handled routine queries but failed at complex cases and frustrated customers.

Common Misconception

"AI will replace all jobs and cause mass unemployment."

This fear has accompanied every major technology shift:

1810s

Luddites smash looms

1960s

Kennedy warns of automation

2000s

Robots in factories

2024

AI everywhere

Each time: specific jobs destroyed, new categories created . In 2000, nobody was a social media manager, data scientist, or app developer.

The real challenge: the people who lose jobs are not always the people who get new ones. The transition is the problem.

Key Takeaways

Labor demand = MRP curve. Hire until MRP = wage.

Wages differ due to compensating differentials, human capital, and discrimination.

Inequality is measured by Lorenz curves & Gini. US inequality surged since 1980.

AI automates tasks, not jobs. Combine AI + human judgment to be augmented.

  The labor market is personal. Your education, career, and skill decisions are investments in your human capital.
