Data Structure Notation

Subscript tells you what varies:
\(y_i\) — unit varies, time fixed
\(y_{it}\) — both vary simultaneously
This notation recurs throughout the entire course. y_i in chapters 12-13 for cross-sectional OLS. y_it in chapters 10-14 for panel methods.

Section 2

The Big Mac Index

Purchasing Power Parity in Practice

Purchasing Power Parity

If a Big Mac costs $5.79 in the US and CHF 6.90 in Switzerland, what should the exchange rate be?

\[\text{Implied PPP} = \frac{P_{\text{domestic}}}{P_{\text{US}}} = \frac{6.90}{5.79} = 1.19 \text{ CHF/\$}\]

Actual spot rate: 0.87 CHF/$

Implied > Spot → Swiss franc is overvalued

\[\text{Valuation} = \frac{1.19 - 0.87}{0.87} \times 100 = \mathbf{+37\%}\]

Work this by hand on the board. 6.90 / 5.79 = 1.19. If markets were perfect, 1 dollar buys 1.19 francs. But it actually buys 0.87. The franc buys more than it should — overvalued by 37%.

Worked Example: Three Countries

Country Local Price US Price Implied PPP Spot Rate Valuation
Switzerland CHF 6.90 $5.79 1.19 0.87 +37.0%
Japan ¥450 $5.79 77.72 155.60 −50.1%
Brazil R$22.90 $5.79 3.95 5.07 −22.1%

Why the differences? Non-tradeable inputs: rent, wages, utilities, taxes. Swiss Big Macs are expensive because Swiss labor is expensive — not because the franc is 37% too strong.

After working Switzerland, show Japan: 450/5.79 = 77.72 implied but actual 155.60. Yen deeply undervalued. Ask: why is Switzerland expensive and Japan cheap?

The Economist's Workbench

DataFrames: Spreadsheets with Rules

Like a spreadsheet: rows and columns

Unlike a spreadsheet:

  • Enforces types (prices can't contain names)
  • Operations are reproducible (code, not clicks)
  • Handles millions of rows
# Label-based selection
swiss = latest.loc[
  latest["name"] == "Switzerland",
  "dollar_price"
]

# Position-based selection
first3 = latest.iloc[:3, :5]

Key Concept — Tidy Data (Wickham 2014): each variable = column, each observation = row. If your data isn't tidy, reshape first.

.loc uses names, .iloc uses numbers. Hammer this distinction — it's the #1 pandas confusion for beginners. More in lab.

Missing Data

When Gaps Tell a Story

Russia Disappears

Filter the Big Mac Index for Russia:

  • 2000–2022: complete data
  • Feb 2022: invasion of Ukraine
  • Post-2022: nothing

The disappearance correlates with the event that affects exchange rates — the very variable we're measuring.

# When did Russia leave?
russia = df.loc[
  df["name"] == "Russia"
]
print(russia["date"].max())
# 2022-01-01
# After Feb 2022 → MNAR
Draw timeline on board: 2000 → 2022 = data. 2022 → now = gone. Ask: Is this random? No. It's the invasion. And the invasion changed exchange rates — the variable we're measuring. The missingness is informative.

Rubin's Three Mechanisms (1976)

MCAR — Missing Completely At Random

Server crash deletes responses. Unrelated to any variable. Safe to drop.

MAR — Missing At Random

Young people skip salary surveys, but conditional on age, missingness is unrelated to salary. Addressable by conditioning.

MNAR — Missing Not At Random

High earners refuse to report salary because it's high. The value itself determines missingness. Dangerous — bias cannot be fully corrected.

Concrete anchors: MCAR = dog ate the survey. MAR = young people threw it away but we know who's young. MNAR = rich people threw it away BECAUSE they're rich — and richness is what we're measuring.

Missing Data Patterns

Green = data present, Red = data missing. Note Russia's abrupt cutoff post-2022.

Point at Russia: clean break at 2022. That's MNAR — geopolitical signal. Venezuela and Argentina have sporadic gaps — different mechanism (likely economic crises). Each gap tells a story.

Back to the ONS Crisis

Response rates: 97.5% → 83.6%

Is this MCAR, MAR, or MNAR?

If non-respondents are disproportionately high-income workers who value privacy…

And the survey measures employment and wages…

This is MNAR.

Published wage growth was likely overstated. The Bank of England may have kept interest rates too high, too long.

This is the narrative payoff. The hook from minute 4 resolves here. Pause for effect after revealing MNAR. The measurement failure didn't produce a footnote — it may have altered the trajectory of an economy.

Class Poll

A university surveys graduates about starting salaries.
Graduates earning above $200K are least likely to respond. This is:

A. MCAR — random
B. MAR — depends on year
C. MNAR — value determines missingness

C — MNAR. The probability of responding depends on the salary itself. Any published "average starting salary" is biased downward.

Wrap-Up

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

Data is built, not given. Every dataset reflects measurement decisions.

Three structures. Cross-section compares. Time series tracks. Panel does both.

PPP is computable. Big Mac Index turns theory into a pandas exercise.

Missing data = information. MCAR/MAR/MNAR determines if your analysis is biased.

Reproducibility is non-negotiable. Set random_state=42. Document your code. Make it verifiable.
