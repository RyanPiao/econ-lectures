An Index Is a Compression Algorithm

A grocery store carries 30,000 products. The CPI compresses them into one number. Every compression discards information. The question is always: what did you throw away?

"How much would it cost TODAY to buy EXACTLY what a typical consumer bought in the base year?" The quantities are frozen — that's simplicity AND the source of substitution bias.

The Real CPI: 80,000 Prices

BLS Field Operation:

  • 400 field economists visit stores in person
  • 23,000 retail establishments sampled monthly
  • Housing = 36.2% of total weight
  • Hedonic regression for quality adjustment

Substitution Bias

When beef prices spike, consumers switch to chicken. The CPI keeps counting beef at the old quantity.

Boskin Commission (1996): CPI overstates inflation by 0.8–1.6 pp/year

Dow Jones: Price-weighted, not cost-weighted. $200 stock = 4× the weight of $50 stock. Divisor ≈ 0.152 fixes stock splits.

Every index is a designed artifact.

Key insight: the Dow and CPI use fundamentally different weighting. Same items, different weights, different conclusions. The weighting scheme IS the measurement decision.

Real vs. Nominal

Deflating History

Who Is Richer?

$5,000

Your grandfather, 1970

CPI = 38.8

$55,000

You, 2024

CPI = 314

\[\text{Real}_{2024} = \frac{\$5{,}000}{38.8} \times 314 = \$40{,}464\]

You’re 36% richer in real terms — not 11× richer

The gap between 1,000% and 36% is money illusion

Numbers before abstraction. Do the computation on the board FIRST, then show the formula. Students need to see $5,000 become $40,464 before they'll internalize the formula.

The Deflation Formula

THE MOST COMMON OPERATION IN APPLIED ECONOMICS

\[\text{Real Value}_t = \frac{\text{Nominal Value}_t}{CPI_t} \times 100\]

Nominal = current dollars
The number on the paycheck

Real = constant purchasing power
What the money can actually buy

Never compare monetary amounts across time without deflating. “Median income hit an all-time high” is vacuous without this step.

This formula returns in Chapters 4, 12, 16, and 20. Every monetary variable in a regression must be deflated first. Using nominal wages as a dependent variable produces spurious trends driven by inflation.

The Fifty-Year Divergence

1973 break: Oil shocks + end of Bretton Woods. Real wages declined for 20 years — invisible in nominal terms.

2020 break: Pandemic stimulus + tight labor. Gain partially eroded by 2021-22 inflation spike.

KEY MOMENT. When the two lines appear — pause. Let it sink in. Five decades of nominal growth, zero real progress. "Money illusion hides structural change."

Pair Computation

CPI was 130.7 in 1990. You earned $10/hr.
CPI was 44.4 in 1973. Someone earned $4/hr.

$7.65

1973: $4 ÷ 44.4 × 100

The 1973 worker had higher purchasing power despite earning less than half in nominal terms!

2 min to calculate, 3 min to share. This is real-wage stagnation in miniature. Transition: "Deflation adjusts across time. In Ch 1, PPP adjusted across space. Same formula, different axis."

Your First Reusable Tool

def deflate_series(nominal, cpi, base_year=2020):
    base_cpi = cpi.loc[cpi.index.year == base_year].mean()
    aligned = pd.DataFrame({"nominal": nominal,
                             "cpi": cpi}).dropna()
    real = (aligned["nominal"] / aligned["cpi"]) * base_cpi
    return real

You’ll use this function again in Chapters 4, 12, 16, and 20. Every monetary variable in a regression must be deflated first.

Not live-coding — just a bridge. Point: "This is the translation of the formula into code. You'll build and use it in the lab."

When Indexes Fail

Goodhart’s Law

Goodhart’s Law

“When a measure becomes a target,
it ceases to be a good measure.”

— Charles Goodhart, 1975

MECHANISM 1

Optimization Against
CPI → Social Security → political pressure on methodology

MECHANISM 2

Gaming
Inflate the number without improving reality

MECHANISM 3

Obsolescence
Basket lags reality (streaming, gig economy)

"Ask students for examples of numbers that stopped being useful once people tried to maximize them — GPA, test scores, follower counts. Let 3-4 respond."

Wells Fargo: “Going for Gr-eight”

Target: 8 financial products per household
Method: Employees opened 3.5 million unauthorized accounts
Duration: 2002–2016 (14 years)

The metric went up. The behavior it measured was actively destroyed.

Consequences

  • $3 billion in fines
  • 5,300+ employees fired
  • CEO resignation
  • Fed capped asset growth (unprecedented)

Cross-sell ratio was reasonable in principle. The problem: elevation from diagnostic indicator to optimization target.

This is Mechanism 2 — gaming. The counter-metric that could have caught it: count only customer-INITIATED account openings.

The Counter-Metric Pattern

Meta: DAU/MAU optimized until it measured notification spam, not engagement. Fix: “Meaningful Social Interactions” (MSI) as a guardrail.

The fix is not a better single metric — it’s a measurement system where one metric watches another.

Every evaluation metric in Ch 18 (accuracy, F1, AUC) is an index subject to Goodhart's Law. Counter-metrics become a design principle.

Key Takeaways

1. An index compresses many measurements into one number. The compression choices shape every conclusion.

2. The Laspeyres CPI overstates inflation due to substitution bias — fixed baskets ignore consumer switching.

3. Never compare monetary amounts across time without deflating. Nominal ≠ Real.

4. Goodhart’s Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be good. The fix is counter-metrics.

5. Every model you build (Chapters 12–24) takes index numbers as inputs. If the index is broken, the model inherits the break.

5 key sentences. Read each one slowly. The fifth is the bridge to the rest of the course.
