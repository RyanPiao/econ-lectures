SECTION 1

From Supervised to Unsupervised

Why remove \(y\)?

Transition: In every chapter since 12, you had labels. What changes when the labels disappear?

The Fundamental Shift

Supervised (Ch 12–21)

  • Features \(\mathbf{X}\) and target \(y\)
  • Learn \(\hat{f}: \mathbf{X} \to y\)
  • Evaluate: \(R^2\), accuracy, MASE
  • Goal: predict

Unsupervised (Ch 22)

  • Features \(\mathbf{X}\) only — no \(y\)
  • Discover structure in \(\mathbf{X}\)
  • Evaluate: WCSS, silhouette
  • Goal: discover

Two families of unsupervised methods:

  • Clustering — partition observations into groups (K-Means)
  • Dimensionality reduction — compress features (PCA)
Draw a scatter with two blobs on the board — "How many groups?" Everyone sees two. Draw a gradient — no separation. "How many now?" The answer depends on how many you impose. Clustering is a modeling choice, not a fact.

Class Poll

Without \(y\), which can you no longer compute?

A) Mean of \(X\)
B) \(R^2\)
C) Correlation between features
D) Standard deviation of features

B) \(R^2\) — requires comparing predictions to actual \(y\). Means, correlations, and standard deviations only use \(\mathbf{X}\). Unsupervised learning needs entirely different evaluation metrics.

SECTION 2

K-Means: Lloyd's Algorithm

The assignment-update cycle

The simplest and most widely used clustering algorithm. Two steps, repeat until convergence.

Lloyd's Algorithm — Intuition

Imagine 160 people in a room. Divide them into 4 groups based on 20 characteristics. No labels.

Step 0: Initialize

Place K "representatives" randomly

Step 1: Assign

Each observation → nearest centroid

Step 2: Update

Recompute centroid = mean

Repeat Steps 1 & 2 until no observation changes cluster

Before showing the figure, sketch a 2D example on the board. Draw ~12 points, place two centroids randomly, walk through one iteration of assignment and update. Students should see the logic before seeing the polished version.

Lloyd's Algorithm — Formal Treatment

Assignment Step

\(c_i = \arg\min_{k} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mu_k\|^2\)

Each point goes to its nearest centroid (squared Euclidean distance)

Update Step

\(\mu_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|} \sum_{i \in C_k} \mathbf{x}_i\)

Centroid = mean of assigned observations

Objective Function — WCSS

\(\text{WCSS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i \in C_k} \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mu_k\|^2\)

Lower WCSS = tighter clusters. Each iteration reduces or maintains WCSS ⇒ guaranteed convergence (to a local minimum).

Walk through each equation. The key insight: the update step computes a mean. That single word — mean — is the key to understanding both the power and the fragility of K-Means.

K-Means in Action

Left: random initialization. Center: after one iteration. Right: convergence. Centroids stabilize; so do the groups.

Point to each panel. "Random start — assignments changed, centroids moved — convergence. Notice: the update step computes a mean. That's the vulnerability we'll exploit in the next slide."

Initialization Matters

Common Misconception

"K-Means always finds the best clustering."

K-Means converges to a local minimum of WCSS — not the global minimum. Different random initializations → different results.

K-Means++ (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007)

  • First centroid: random
  • Each subsequent: probability ∝ squared distance from nearest existing centroid
  • Ensures centroids are spread apart ⇒ dramatically reduces bad local minima
  • Default in scikit-learn (init='k-means++')
This is why scikit-learn runs the algorithm multiple times (n_init) and returns the best result. K-Means++ makes the starting position much better, but you still need multiple restarts.

Callback to Chapter 4

The centroid is a mean. The mean has breakdown point \(1/n\).

Quick calculation:

A cluster of 50 countries has avg GDP/capita = $12,000.

A microstate with GDP = $5,000,000 joins.

\(\mu_{\text{new}} = \frac{50 \times 12{,}000 + 5{,}000{,}000}{51} \approx \$109{,}804\)

One observation moved the centroid by ~10×. The other 50 countries may now reassign to a different cluster.

Robust alternative: K-Medoids (PAM) uses the medoid (actual data point closest to center) instead of the mean — just as the median replaces the mean for skewed data.

Ask: "What's the unsupervised analog of using the median instead of the mean?" Target: K-Medoids. Same tradeoff, same reasoning, new domain. The centroid's fragility is exactly the mean's fragility from Chapter 4.

Standardization Is NOT Optional

K-Means uses Euclidean distance — treats all dimensions equally. But economic variables have wildly different scales:

$300 – $120,000

GDP per capita range

25 – 65

Gini index range

\(z_{ij} = \frac{x_{ij} - \bar{x}_j}{s_j}\)    (same z-score from Chapter 8)

After standardization, 1 unit = 1 standard deviation in any feature. All variables contribute equally.

Without StandardScaler, you are clustering on GDP per capita alone — the other 9 variables are noise.

This is the single most common error in the lab. Emphasize: "If you forget StandardScaler, your results are garbage. Not 'slightly different' — fundamentally wrong."

In Code

from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans

# Step 1: Standardize (MANDATORY)
scaler = StandardScaler()
X_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(df[feature_columns])

# Step 2: Fit K-Means with K=4
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=4, init='k-means++',
                n_init='auto', random_state=42)
df['cluster'] = kmeans.fit_predict(X_scaled)
  • StandardScaler — mean 0, variance 1 (mandatory)
  • init='k-means++' — smart initialization (default)
  • n_init='auto' — multiple restarts, keep best
  • random_state=42 — reproducibility
Show this code on screen. Three things to emphasize: (1) StandardScaler is not optional; (2) random_state for reproducibility; (3) n_init='auto' handles the local minima problem. Ask: "Why K=4? Is that the right number?" — seeds the next section.

SECTION 3

Choosing \(K\)

The elbow method and silhouette analysis

This is the central hyperparameter problem — analogous to choosing lambda in LASSO or B in Random Forests.

The Elbow Method

Plot WCSS vs. \(K\) for \(K = 1, 2, \ldots, 10\).

  • More clusters always reduces WCSS
  • Look for the "elbow" where improvement slows
  • Before elbow: real structure
  • After elbow: chasing noise

Problem

The elbow is often ambiguous in real data — WCSS decreases smoothly without a sharp inflection.

Show the elbow plot. Point to where the curve bends — is it K=3 or K=4? Often ambiguous. That's why we need a second method.

The Silhouette Score

\(s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i), b(i))}\)

\(a(i)\) = cohesion

Mean distance to own cluster

\(b(i)\) = separation

Mean distance to nearest other cluster

\(s(i) \in [-1, 1]\)

Near 1 = good, 0 = boundary, <0 = wrong cluster

Choose \(K\) that maximizes mean silhouette. Unlike the elbow, the silhouette gives a principled maximum — but the two methods can disagree.

Ch 18 connection: Silhouette score is the unsupervised analog of AUC — measures separation without ground-truth labels.

Walk through the formula. a(i) = how tight is this point within its cluster. b(i) = how far from the next-best cluster. If b much larger than a, the point is well-placed. If a is larger, it might be in the wrong cluster.

Choosing \(K\) Is Bias-Variance

\(K = 1\)

High bias
Ignores all structure

↔

Optimal \(K\)
balances both

\(K = n\)

High variance
Memorizes every point

Same tension from Chapter 15 — in a new setting.

No single "correct" \(K\). Domain knowledge provides the final answer.

This connects directly to Chapter 15. Too few clusters = underfit. Too many = overfit. The elbow and silhouette give guidance; the economist gives the final answer.

Class Poll

K-Means with K=2 gives silhouette = 0.45. You try K=4. What happens?

A) Silhouette will definitely increase
B) Silhouette will definitely decrease
C) It depends — could go either way
D) Silhouette is always the same

C) Adding clusters sometimes improves separation (real subgroups) and sometimes worsens it (splitting natural groups). This is bias-variance in action.

If students pick A, address: "More clusters does NOT automatically mean better separation. You might split a natural group in two, making both halves less cohesive."

SECTION 4

PCA: Dimensionality Reduction

Compressing 10 dimensions to 2

We've clustered 160 countries on 10 indicators. But how do you visualize 10 dimensions? PCA finds the best 2D projection.

PCA — The "Best Photograph"

Imagine photographing a 3D sculpture. Each photo is a 2D projection — it captures some shape and loses the rest.

The best photo is taken from the angle showing the most variation in form.

PCA finds that "best angle" mathematically: the linear combination capturing maximum variance.

\(\mathbf{w}_1 = \arg\max_{\|\mathbf{w}\|=1} \text{Var}(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{w})\)

The sculpture analogy makes PCA intuitive. PC1 = the most informative angle. PC2 = the second most informative angle, perpendicular to the first. Together they give you the best 2D view of your 10D data.

How Much Information Do We Keep?

\(\text{EVR}_j = \frac{\lambda_j}{\sum_{l=1}^p \lambda_l}\)    (fraction of total variance in component \(j\))

In development data, PC1 + PC2 typically explain 60–80% of total variance.

Why so much? Many indicators are correlated: rich countries have high life expectancy, low infant mortality, high internet access simultaneously.

from sklearn.decomposition import PCA

pca = PCA(n_components=2,
          random_state=42)
X_pca = pca.fit_transform(X_scaled)

# Typically:
# PC1: ~45% variance
# PC1+PC2: ~65-75% variance
The explained variance ratio tells you how much of the story each component tells. If 75% is captured by two components, you're keeping most of the information while reducing from 10 to 2 dimensions.

Country Clusters in PCA Space

PC1 ≈ "general development" axis. PC2 captures secondary variation (trade openness, resource dependence). Resist over-interpretation — PCA finds variance, economists supply meaning.

Point to exemplar countries. "Norway and the US cluster together — high development. Ethiopia in a different cluster. Where's India? Brazil?" Students see recognizable groups. Ask: "Does PC1 have an economic interpretation?" Walk through loadings briefly.

SECTION 5

The Development Club

Algorithms vs. institutions

Now we bring it all together: the complete unsupervised pipeline applied to classifying economies. And we compare the result to what the World Bank does.

World Bank vs. K-Means

World Bank

  • 1 variable: GNI per capita
  • 4 fixed thresholds
  • Deterministic, transparent
  • Updated annually (SDR deflator)
  • Determines IDA lending eligibility

K-Means

  • 10 variables: GDP, health, education, trade…
  • Data-driven groups
  • Statistically tighter
  • Changes with features or K
  • Captures multivariate nuance

Adjusted Rand Index ≈ 0.70 — broad agreement, but meaningful discrepancies at boundaries. Countries like Argentina, Turkey, Malaysia sit on cluster edges.

The comparison is the pedagogical climax. Neither system is "right." The World Bank trades nuance for transparency. K-Means trades interpretability for statistical tightness. When would you prefer each?

What Does Each Cluster Look Like?

Cluster centroids in original (unstandardized) scale. Clusters 0 and 3 show clear separation; Clusters 1 and 2 overlap on several dimensions — these are the boundary cases.

Point to the key differences: "Cluster 0 = low GDP, low life expectancy, high infant mortality. Cluster 3 = the opposite. But Clusters 1 and 2 overlap — these are where K-Means and the World Bank disagree most."

"Which classification system is better for understanding global development?"

  • Then switch sides.
3 minutes
Walk around during the discussion. Listen for "transparency" and "simplicity" for World Bank, "statistical tightness" and "multivariate" for K-Means. Both are correct — that's the point.

Debrief: Neither Is "Right"

World Bank is better when…

  • Transparency matters (any government can verify)
  • Stability matters (thresholds don't jump around)
  • Administrative decisions (lending eligibility)

K-Means is better when…

  • You want to discover latent structure
  • Multiple dimensions matter
  • Research questions, not policy decisions

Brief alternatives:

  • DBSCAN — density-based, no \(K\) required, classifies outliers as noise
  • Hierarchical — dendrogram shows structure at every possible \(K\)
  • GMMs — soft assignment (probabilities: 70% Cluster 1, 30% Cluster 2)
Both answer different questions. The World Bank system is univariate and transparent. K-Means is multivariate and data-driven. This is the fundamental tension in applied data science — statistical optimality vs. interpretability.
