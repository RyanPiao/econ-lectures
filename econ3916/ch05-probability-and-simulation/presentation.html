Three Ways to Think About Probability

Classical

Count equally likely outcomes

P(six) = 1/6

Breaks in economics — "equally likely" is rarely defensible

Frequentist

Long-run relative frequency

% of winning lottery tickets

Awkward for one-time events — "P(recession in 2027)?"

Bayesian

Degree of belief, updated with evidence

Start 50% → update with data

Handles one-off events naturally

All three obey the same mathematical rules. The axioms are shared — the interpretation differs.

Quick overview — spend ~5 min here. Students often default to frequentist. Push: "What's the long-run frequency of a recession in 2027? It only happens once." This motivates Bayesian thinking.

The Complement Trick

Problem: What is P(at least one six in 4 rolls)?

Wrong approach:

\(1/6 + 1/6 + 1/6 + 1/6 = 4/6\)

Exceeds 1 for 7+ rolls!

Complement:

\(P(\text{at least one}) = 1 - P(\text{none})\)

\(= 1 - (5/6)^4 = 1 - 0.482 = \mathbf{0.518}\)

This trick appears constantly in risk analysis: "P(at least one default)" = 1 − "P(zero defaults)"

Work on board. Ask: "Why can I multiply?" — independence. Catch the 4/6 error explicitly — students make this mistake consistently.

Pause after the three stats. Let students absorb. Then reveal the "your instinct is wrong" line. This creates cognitive dissonance that motivates Bayes' Theorem.

Bayes' Theorem: The Learning Rule

Step 1: Total probability of being flagged

\(P(\text{Flag}) = 0.99 \times 0.001 + 0.02 \times 0.999 = 0.021\)

Step 2: Apply Bayes'

\(P(\text{Fraud}|\text{Flag}) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.001}{0.021} = \mathbf{0.047}\)

Only 4.7% of flagged transactions are fraud. 95% are false alarms.

Work this on the board in three steps. After 0.047, pause. Students expect ~99%. The surprise is the pedagogical payoff. Then: "A 99% accurate algorithm produces 95% false alarms when the base rate is low."

The Normal Distribution Lies

Fat tails and the shape of real uncertainty

When "Never" Happens on Tuesday

Under a normal distribution, a 5% daily S&P 500 drop should occur once every 14,000 years.

In reality, it happened six times between 2000 and 2024.

That's a 600× discrepancy. The normal distribution systematically underestimates extreme events.

"A 5% daily drop should happen once every 14,000 years." Pause. "It happened six times since 2000." Let the contradiction land. "When the model says 'never' and reality says 'Tuesday,' the model is wrong."

Normal vs. Fat-Tailed Distributions

Normal: Thin tails. Extreme values are "impossible."

Student-t: Fat tails. Extreme values happen regularly.

Don't derive the PDF formula. Students need to recognize the shape, not reproduce the math. Key message: "Always plot before assuming normality."

Answer: C — 14,000 Years

The normal distribution predicts ~14,000 years. But it happened 6 times in 24 years — a 600× discrepancy.

This is why financial regulators moved from VaR (assumes normality) to Expected Shortfall (doesn't). The fat tails of real data make rare events far more common than the bell curve predicts.

Rule: "When the model says 'never' and reality says 'Tuesday,' the model is wrong."

Monte Carlo Simulation

When equations fail, simulate

What Is Monte Carlo Simulation?

When math is intractable, generate thousands of synthetic outcomes and count results.

Federal Reserve

250,000 draws for bank stress tests

Swiss Re

Hurricane simulations for catastrophe insurance

Climate Economists

Damage function uncertainty for Social Cost of Carbon

Origin: Named after the Monte Carlo Casino. In the 1940s, physicists Ulam and von Neumann estimated nuclear physics integrals by random sampling instead of solving equations.

The Law of Large Numbers

Monte Carlo works because of LLN: as independent draws grow, the sample mean converges to the expected value.

\(\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} E[X]\)

Convergence rate: Error \(\propto 1/\sqrt{n}\)

To halve error → 4× simulations. Slow — but works in any dimension.

Monte Carlo estimate of π converges to true value

"This chart is LLN in action. The estimate is noisy at first but converges. Notice the 1/sqrt(n) rate — the wiggles get smaller, but slowly." Connect: "The random_state=42 you've been setting since Ch1 is the seed for this engine."

VaR vs. Expected Shortfall

Value at Risk (VaR)

"Maximum loss at X% confidence"

\(\text{VaR}_q = -F^{-1}(1-q)\)

Tells you the door, not what's behind it

Expected Shortfall (ES)

"Average loss when things go badly"

\(\text{ES}_q = -E[R \mid R \leq -\text{VaR}_q]\)

Captures tail shape — Basel III standard since Jan 2025

Draw on board: histogram, vertical line = VaR, shade the tail = ES. "VaR tells you the door; ES tells you what's behind it." Basel FRTB: "In January 2025, the EU/UK made ES the standard. US follows in 2025-2026."

Diminishing returns: 10,000 sims → ~1% error. Getting to 0.5% needs 40,000. The Fed uses 250,000 because billion-dollar capital decisions demand precision.

Key Takeaways

Distributions formalize uncertainty. Every statistic assumes a distribution. Making it explicit lets you reason about risk.

Bayes' Theorem is a learning rule. 99% accurate ≠ 99% reliable. Always check the base rate.

Monte Carlo replaces intractable math. LLN guarantees convergence at \(O(1/\sqrt{n})\). Trade elegance for computation.

ES replaced VaR because VaR ignores tail severity. Basel III FRTB made ES the global standard (Jan 2025).

The normal distribution is a useful lie. Always plot before assuming normality.
