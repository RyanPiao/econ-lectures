Section 2

TF-IDF

Importance = Frequency × Rarity

Term Frequency – Inverse Document Frequency

\[\text{TF-IDF}(t, d, D) = \underbrace{\text{TF}(t, d)}_{\text{frequency in this doc}} \times \underbrace{\log\frac{|D|}{df(t)}}_{\text{rarity across corpus}}\]

TF(t, d)

How often word t appears in this document

|D|

Total documents in corpus (260 FOMC meetings)

df(t)

Documents containing word t (corpus-wide commonness)

High TF-IDF = frequent here, rare everywhere else. Low TF-IDF = ubiquitous word.

Walk through the formula slowly. TF captures local frequency. IDF captures global rarity. The product rewards words that are distinctive to a specific document. This is the single most important formula in the chapter.

Worked Example: January 2023 FOMC

"inflation"

TF = 15 / 5,000 = 0.003

df = 40 of 260 meetings

IDF = log(260/40) ≈ 1.87

TF-IDF = 0.0056 ✓

"committee"

TF = 25 / 5,000 = 0.005

df = 260 of 260 meetings

IDF = log(260/260) = log(1) = 0

TF-IDF = 0 ✗

"committee" appears more often but carries zero information — information ∝ rarity, not frequency

This is the aha moment. Circle IDF = 0 for "committee." Students physically see why the most frequent words are the least informative. The terracotta banner drives it home: information is proportional to rarity.

In Code: TfidfVectorizer

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer

vectorizer = TfidfVectorizer(
    max_features=1000,       # Keep top 1000 words
    stop_words='english',    # Remove common English words
    ngram_range=(1, 2),      # Bigrams: "interest rate", "labor market"
    sublinear_tf=True,       # 1 + log(tf) — diminishing returns
)

X_tfidf = vectorizer.fit_transform(documents)
# Shape: (260, 1000) — 260 meetings × 1000 features

sublinear_tf: Mentioning "growth" 20 times ≠ 20× more growth-focused. Diminishing marginal information.

Discussion

You count the word "inflation" 15 times in January 2023 FOMC minutes and 12 times in March 2023.

Can you conclude January was more focused on inflation? Why or why not?

Think-pair-share: 2 min with a partner, then 2 min class discussion

Two issues students should identify: (1) document length — 15 in a 15,000-word doc is less concentrated than 12 in 5,000 words. TF normalizes for this. (2) Corpus context — if "inflation" appears in most meetings, even 15 isn't distinctive. IDF handles this.

Discussion Debrief

Problem 1: Document Length

15 mentions in 15,000 words (0.1%) < 12 mentions in 5,000 words (0.24%). TF normalizes for document length.

Problem 2: Corpus Context

If "inflation" appears in 200 of 260 meetings, even 15 mentions is not distinctive. IDF penalizes common words.

TF-IDF handles both problems — it accounts for document-level frequency AND corpus-level rarity.

Sentiment Analysis

Why generic dictionaries fail — and what works

Why Generic Sentiment Fails

The Harvard General Inquirer (1960s) classifies words for everyday English. In financial text, this fails spectacularly:

"liability"

Harvard: NEGATIVE

Reality: accounting term

"tax"

Harvard: NEGATIVE

Reality: line item

"capital"

Harvard: NEGATIVE

Reality: balance sheet

"foreign"

Harvard: NEGATIVE

Reality: geographic

73.8%

of Harvard "negative" words are NOT negative in financial text

Loughran & McDonald (2011), Journal of Finance

Write 73.8% on the board in large letters. "If I gave you a thermometer that read wrong 73.8% of the time, would you use it to decide if you have a fever?" Students laugh — then realize this is exactly what happens with general dictionaries on financial text. This is attenuation bias from Chapter 12.

The Loughran-McDonald Lexicon

Category Examples Words
Negativeloss, impairment, default, litigation, adverse~2,350

Uncertaintyapproximate, contingent, possible, uncertain~290

\[\text{Sent}(d) = \frac{N_{\text{pos}} - N_{\text{neg}}}{N_{\text{pos}} + N_{\text{neg}} + \varepsilon}\]

Ranges from −1 (all negative) to +1 (all positive)

Move quickly through the table — students don't need to memorize the counts. The key insight: 6.6x more negative words than positive (2,350 vs 354) reflects that companies have many more ways to disclose bad news than good news. The formula is straightforward — you'll implement it in the lab.
