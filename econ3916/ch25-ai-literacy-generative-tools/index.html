Section 1

From Traditional ML to Language Models

Transition to the first content section. We'll cover LLMs, stochastic parrots, and embeddings.

LLMs Are Conditional Probability Machines

$$P(w_{t+1} \mid w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_t)$$

Given all previous tokens, predict the next token

No fact database. No verification. Just pattern matching at scale

When ChatGPT writes "CPI was 3.2%," it's producing a high-probability token sequence — the number may or may not be correct

This is it. The entire foundation of ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — they predict the next word. Profoundly simple and profoundly powerful. But zero built-in fact-checking.

Next-Token Prediction in Action

The model assigns probabilities to every possible next token. "Rates" is most likely — but the model doesn't know what the Fed actually did.

Walk through the figure. The model picks 'rates' because that's the most common completion in its training data. It could be wrong. It has no way to check.

Stochastic Parrots

Bender et al. (2021): LLMs produce fluent text by stitching together statistical patterns without understanding meaning

"The unemployment rate fell to 3.4% in January 2023"

↑ Not a verified fact. A high-probability token sequence.

Like a parrot reproducing speech without comprehension

The number might be correct. It might not. The model cannot tell the difference.

The parrot metaphor is precise. Students will remember it. Key point: the model has no truth-checking mechanism. It's not lying — it's not capable of lying. It's generating patterns.

From TF-IDF to Embeddings

Ch 23: TF-IDF (Sparse)

One dimension per word
Mostly zeros
No meaning captured

Ch 25: Embeddings (Dense)

384–1536 dimensions
Every dimension carries info
Similar meanings cluster

$$\text{embed}(\text{"inflation expectations"}) \approx \text{embed}(\text{"price forecasts"})$$

Connect to Ch 23: same idea — text as numbers — with a much richer representation. The TF-IDF vectors were sparse; embeddings are dense and capture meaning.

Sparse vs Dense Representations

Left: TF-IDF scatters documents randomly because it can't capture meaning. Right: embeddings cluster similar documents together. This is why RAG works — you can find relevant documents by meaning, not just keywords.

How RAG Works: 4 Stages

Same FOMC corpus from Ch 23 — now as a RAG knowledge base instead of TF-IDF vectors

Walk through each stage with FOMC example. Stage 1: split minutes into paragraphs. Stage 2: each paragraph becomes a vector. Stage 3: embed your question, find 5 most similar paragraphs. Stage 4: LLM summarizes those specific paragraphs.

Retrieval: Finding Relevant Documents

$$\text{retrieved} = \arg\max_{d \in \mathcal{D}} \; \text{sim}(\text{embed}(q), \text{embed}(d))$$

$q$ = your question

$\mathcal{D}$ = all document chunks in the vector database

$\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ = cosine similarity (how close are the vectors?)

Instead of making things up → the model reads YOUR documents first

This is the key equation. The model doesn't hallucinate about the Fed because it's reading the actual FOMC minutes, not generating from memory. It's the difference between citing a source and answering from memory on an exam.

Section 4

Evaluating AI-Generated Analysis

The payoff section. How do you verify what AI tells you?

The 4-Step Verification Framework

1. Source Verification — Does the claim cite a verifiable source? No source → unverified.

2. Consistency Check — Does it pass a sanity test? "15% unemployment" → your domain knowledge flags this.

3. Adversarial Testing — Ask the same question differently. Different answers → at least one is wrong.

4. Reproduction — Re-run the analysis yourself. AI accelerates; it doesn't replace.

These are the same habits from 24 chapters of this course — applied to AI output

Source verification = citing your data source. Consistency checks = sanity-checking coefficients. Adversarial testing = robustness checks. Reproduction = reproduction. You already know how to do this.
